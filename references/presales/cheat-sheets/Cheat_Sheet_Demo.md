# Cheat Sheet — Demo

---

## Before the Demo

- Find out the **Top 3-5 things** the customer cares about. Prepare and present top-down.
- Keep the first discovery to **30 minutes max**. After 25 min, offer a second session — this shows if they are serious.
- Understand your audience. Tailor the demo to their needs, goals, and pain points.
- Plan and rehearse the demo. Test your screen sharing, slides, and flow.
- Test the software before the call. Fix any issues in advance.
- Collect resources: customer testimonials, case studies, competitive info.
- Prepare for technical and functional questions. Know what the software can and cannot do.
- Send a prep email with the agenda, meeting details, and notes on recording and webcam.
- Set up your home office for remote sessions.

---

## During the Demo

- Use **Tell-Show-Tell**. Start with the most important topic. Stay focused.
- Use real-world examples. Show how the software works in their industry.
- Encourage participation. Ask questions and involve the audience.
- Use persona-based use cases. Say "I'm Kevin, I do..." — avoid saying "you can" or "you should."
- Use the ASD to take notes and track questions from the chat.
- Pause and ask questions to keep attention.
- Take a break at least every 45-60 minutes. Use a break timer.
- Turn on your webcam. Ask participants to do the same.
- Reserve at least 10 minutes at the end for questions, a mutual action plan, and next steps.

---

## After the Demo

- Call each participant individually. Ask what was good and what is missing. Best done by the ASD.
- Share the recording and materials via Paperflite.
- Answer all open questions from the demo.
- Schedule a follow-up call to discuss next steps.

---

## Presenting Remote

- Look at the camera — treat it as the eyes of your client.
- Use open-palm gestures to appear trustworthy.
- Center yourself in frame with equal space on both sides.
- Slow down your movements so they are easy to follow on camera.
- Use a solid-colored background. Avoid distractions.
- Dress professionally. Establish credibility.
- Ensure contrast between your clothing, hair, and background.
- Address one person at a time for a more personal feel.
- Avoid black clothing — it can distort depth on video.
- Avoid stripes and check patterns — they can distort on camera.
- Keep notes close to the camera so you don't break eye contact.
- Define roles and set up a back channel with your producer.
- Do a tech rehearsal before the session starts.
- Do not read from a script. Use your own words.
- Memorize key lines such as value propositions.
- Join early. Upload content and test tools before participants arrive.
- Manage chat, participant entry, and support during the session.
- Record the session. Debrief and discuss improvements afterwards.
- Review recordings regularly. Pick one area to improve. Rehearse.
- Use a reliable internet connection and good audio-visual equipment.

---

## Presenting Onsite

- Build genuine rapport through eye contact and authentic smiles.
- Listen actively. Adjust based on audience reactions.
- Stand confidently. Use gestures with intent.
- Keep slides simple and visually clean.
- Involve the audience with questions and interactive tools.
- Use breakout discussions for deeper engagement.
- Plan timed breaks for energy and content digestion.
- Include networking breaks during longer sessions.
- Handle interruptions calmly and professionally.
- Vary your tone, pitch, and volume to maintain attention.
- Tailor content to the specific audience.
- Practice until you know your content completely.
- Be open to feedback. Use it to improve.
- Adapt to audience interests as needed.
- Dress appropriately for the event and audience.
- Use storytelling to make the presentation memorable.
- Use props wisely to reinforce key points.
- Check all equipment before you start.
- Gauge the audience mood before the presentation begins.
- End with a clear summary and encourage further interaction.
