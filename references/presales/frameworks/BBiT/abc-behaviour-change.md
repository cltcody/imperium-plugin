# ABC: Understanding and Changing Behaviour

## What It Is

ABC is a tool for understanding and changing behaviour, your own or other people's. Behaviour isn't a mystery. Like everything else, it's cause and effect.

People do what they do based on what happens to them when they do it, from experience or expectation. Positive outcomes make a behaviour more likely; negative ones make it less likely. We mostly do this subconsciously.

ABC stands for the three parts:
- **Antecedent:** the trigger or situation that prompts a behaviour. It doesn't *cause* a specific behaviour, it just creates the opportunity. (An orange traffic light prompts a choice; some slow down, some speed up.)
- **Behaviour:** the action a person actually does. Not the outcome or result.
- **Consequence:** what happens to the person as a result. This is what drives which behaviour they choose next time.

If you can understand a behaviour, you can predict it, and then you can change it. Almost every solution from the other thinking tools needs a behaviour change to actually happen.

## Why It Matters

We like to think we act purely on logic. If that were true, we'd all be fit, healthy, skilled, and free of bad habits. We're not. So "they just won't do it" or "I agreed to it but nothing changed" needs explaining. Usually the answer is in the consequences, or a missing antecedent.

If you're ever stuck on how this works, think of kids or dogs. A dog learns to sit for a treat. A kid tidies up for ice cream. It feels obvious there. The same logic applies to adults, just more subtly (a compliment when someone does the dishes makes them do it more).

The one crucial rule: **the only consequences that matter are the ones that impact the person doing the behaviour, as they perceive them.** This is where most workplace frustration comes from. Someone does something "that makes no sense" because the positives go to someone else while they get the drawbacks. A tedious new quality process might be good for the business but a net negative for the person doing it, so they quietly do it less.

## What You Should Do

### Step 1: Analyse the behaviour

Understand it before you change it. Build a table for the behaviour.

1. **Behaviour.** What do you see happening that you want to understand? Make it a behaviour, not an outcome. People control their actions, not always their results.
2. **Antecedent.** What triggers it? These can be sensory (seeing the time) or internal (a habit or memory). Changing the trigger can be a powerful lever.
3. **Consequences.** List both positive and negative ones, as they affect the person doing the behaviour. Rate each for impact on a **1 to 5 scale** (1 = almost none, 5 = overwhelming). Most land in the 2 to 4 range.

The impact rating isn't maths. You don't sum positives and negatives. One negative 5 can outweigh fifteen positive 2s. The scale just helps you find the most impactful consequences, which are usually the best ones to change. Aim for a mix of positives and negatives, not a set number.

Analysis sometimes reveals you shouldn't change the behaviour at all. It might be more reasonable than you thought, or a symptom of a deeper problem (punishing someone out of asking for help doesn't remove their need for help). If it's a symptom, use other tools on the root cause.

If there are several behaviours, **analyse each in its own table.** Merging them gets confusing fast, since different antecedents and consequences apply to each.

### Step 2: Adjust a single behaviour

First, define the behaviour and the **magnitude** of change you want (small decrease, big increase, near elimination). This keeps you from under- or overdoing it.

Then shape it by changing the antecedent, the consequences, or both.

**Change antecedents.** Antecedents don't encourage a specific behaviour, but they control how often it has the chance to occur.
- To increase a behaviour that's reinforcing but rarely happens (drinking more water, walking more), add a prompt. Think of the "move" reminder on a fitness watch.
- To decrease a behaviour that only happens in a certain situation, remove the trigger. This is often faster than changing consequences and can give an immediate result.

**Change consequences.** The more common route. Make a behaviour more appealing to increase it, less appealing to decrease it. Three things to plan:
- **Consequence change:** the actual new effect on the person. To increase a behaviour, make it more reinforcing and less punishing. To decrease it, the reverse.
- **How it's implemented:** the practical step that makes the consequence happen. Don't rely on people simply waking up with a new worldview (a smoker suddenly "realising" smoking is bad). If you want a belief shift, have a real plan to cause it.
- **Impact (1 to 5):** how much the new consequence will actually move the behaviour, for *this* person. A useful quality check. Common practices can be weak (an annual bonus is too far removed to change daily behaviour). Match the impact to the magnitude you wanted: a small change needs only low-impact tweaks.

Tip: you can also adjust *existing* consequences, not just add new ones. Changing the severity, visibility, or likelihood of one can turn a low-impact consequence into a high-impact one.

### Step 3: Replace a behaviour (when needed)

Reducing a behaviour leaves a void that another behaviour will fill, possibly an equally bad one. So plan a specific replacement. Replacing is also useful when there's only room for so much of a behaviour and you need to free up space.

Keep an open mind. Don't just label behaviours "good" and "bad." Sometimes you encourage a milder bad habit as a step up from a worse one.

How to do it: run the full analysis and adjustment on **each** behaviour involved. Don't merge them in your head. They can succeed and fail independently, which can give odd results (kids finish homework, but by going to bed late instead of playing fewer video games).

Two sub-types:
- **Directly related behaviours** are two points on one scale (going to bed earlier vs. staying up later). One up means the other down. They share the same consequences, just inverted. You can often use a single table, but check it from both sides.
- **Distinct behaviours** move independently. Spending less on coffee doesn't mean you save the money; you might spend it elsewhere. Most replacements are distinct. Treat every replacement as distinct to be safe; condense to one table only if you find you're writing the same things twice.

## A Note on Ethics

Some of this can feel manipulative. Two things to consider. First, we do it anyway, subconsciously (sugar-coating a request, muttering loudly so your partner feels the guilt). Even calling something "manipulative" is an attempt to change someone's behaviour.

A strong check: **are you willing to tell the person what you did, after you did it?** If yes, you're probably doing it for the right reasons. (You might not tell them in the moment, because being overt can cause kickback.) If you're not comfortable telling them afterward, reconsider your reasons.

## Common Errors

**Consequences in absolute terms.** Judging a consequence by how bad it is in general, not how it impacts the person doing it. Someone throwing rubbish from a car doesn't care that the street looks bad. Their only relevant consequence is getting it out of their car (a positive), plus a small fine risk and maybe their conscience.

**Universal consequences.** Assuming the same thing is positive or negative for everyone. Buying someone a coffee is neutral, or even negative, if they dislike coffee. Public recognition reinforces many people but punishes someone who hates the spotlight, making them do worse to avoid it. Use generic rules as a start point, then adjust to the individual.

**Binary success or failure.** Treating change as all-or-nothing. Some behaviours are deeply ingrained and complete change may be near impossible. Treat it as ongoing improvement. Even a 5% shift in the right direction is a win.

**Outcomes instead of behaviours.** Applying consequences to results people don't fully control. This feels unfair and causes odd shifts. Reward the number of coin flips, not the number of heads.

## Quick Reference

| Part | What it is |
|---|---|
| Antecedent | The trigger that prompts a behaviour (doesn't force it) |
| Behaviour | The action the person actually does (not the outcome) |
| Consequence | What happens to them, which drives future choice |

| Step | What you do |
|---|---|
| 1. Analyse | Table the behaviour, antecedent, and consequences (rate impact 1 to 5) |
| 2. Adjust | Set the magnitude, then change antecedents and/or consequences |
| 3. Replace | Analyse and adjust each behaviour separately when filling a void |
| Golden rule | Only consequences that impact *that person*, as they see them, matter |
| Ethics check | Would you tell them what you did afterward? |
