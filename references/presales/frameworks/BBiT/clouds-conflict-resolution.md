# The Cloud: Resolving Conflicts and Decisions

## What It Is

The cloud is a tool for resolving conflicts. A conflict is any time you have two choices that clash, where doing one means you can't reasonably do the other.

The full name is "evaporating clouds." Like a cloud in the sky, if you look at the conflict long enough with the right information, it disappears. The answer becomes obvious.

You will use this tool more than any other, day to day. Conflicts are everywhere, from tiny choices to major ones.

Three types of conflict:
- **Personal:** You are stuck between two options on your own.
- **Interpersonal:** You and someone else each want a different approach.
- **Policy:** You want to take an action but it runs up against a rule or norm.

## Why It Matters

When you get stuck between two choices, picking one means losing something from the other. You end up worse off, with a negative side effect. The stress of deciding can also freeze you.

The cloud fixes this in two ways:
- It helps you **understand the situation**. More of the right information means better decisions.
- It helps you **resolve the conflict**. You find a solution that gives you the benefit of both sides, not just one.

The key shift: stop asking **"which one?"** and start asking **"why?"** Why do you want each option? Once you know the real reason behind each, you can often get the benefit of both.

Example: "Make the sale" and "ensure quality" are not really in conflict. Only the actions used to reach them clash. Conflicts surface at the action level, not the need level.

## What You Should Do

**Always ask why.** When faced with a choice, ask why you, or someone else, would want it. What is the driver or need behind it?

Protect the "why." Even when you compromise on your preferred approach, do it in a way that keeps the reason for that approach alive.

### Build the cloud (work right to left)

Read connections as "In order to [need], I must [action]."

1. **Start with the two actions in conflict.** These are usually the first thing you write, because they are what you feel stuck on. Example: walk to work vs. drive to work.
2. **Add the need behind each action.** Ask "why do I want this?" Walk to work because I want exercise. Drive to work because I want to save time.
3. **Add the common goal that ties both needs together.** Example: both help me have a good day. The goal matters most when working with others, since it shows why you are both even discussing it.

Use the formal written structure when the conflict is complex or when you need others to agree on it. It documents the dilemma simply and keeps everyone on the same page.

For everyday conflicts, use it in your head. Once you have built a few formally, you can run the same steps informally just by asking the right questions.

### Tip for interpersonal conflicts

Don't jump to defending your side. Ask about their side first.

Example: You want a webinar (so people get live Q&A). They suggest a recorded video (more accessible across time zones). Once both "whys" are clear, it stops being "my side vs. your side" and becomes both of you solving one situation.

### Tip for policy conflicts

Find out why the policy exists, then find a way to get what you want without breaking what the policy protects.

Example: A flight school requires passing the theory exam before flight training. The reason is to avoid interrupted, rusty lessons. Solution: do one intro flight as a one-off, outside the syllabus, so the exam still comes before real training starts. The policy's purpose is protected, and you still get the benefit.

## How to Solve a Cloud

There are three approaches:

1. **It evaporates.** Once the "why" is clear, the answer is obvious. Common with personal clouds, and with interpersonal ones once you learn the other person's need.
2. **A bit of thought.** Ask: how can I keep both needs while picking one action? Often you tweak the action so it serves both sides.
   - Example: Save time by driving, or get exercise by walking. Solution: walk while listening to podcasts or taking calls. Walking now buys back the time too.
   - Example: Webinar vs. video. Solution: send a video to everyone, collect questions, then post a follow-up video answering them. Both needs met.
3. **A formal process.** For tricky or serious conflicts, there is a structured method for creative solutions. That comes later in the course.

Avoid lazy non-solutions like "the customer is always right" or "we are the expert, so our way wins." These just justify one choice. They don't solve the problem.

## Common Errors When Building a Cloud

Use the example "Give a due date vs. don't give a due date." Giving a due date helps make the sale. Not giving one protects quality.

**Actions are not in conflict.** The two actions can both be done at once, so there is nothing to resolve. *Fix:* Word them so they directly clash. You can give a due date or not give one. You can't do both.

**Needs are worded as actions.** "Give employees freedom" is an action, not the real need. *Fix:* Write the true need behind the action. We don't give due dates because the need is "ensure quality."

**Actions are worded as needs.** "Ensure we have a big pipeline" is an outcome, not something you can do right now. *Fix:* Write a real action. "Give a due date" is something you can do immediately.

**Action doesn't violate the opposite need.** If one action doesn't actually harm the other side, there is no real conflict. *Fix:* Check that each action genuinely impacts the need on the other side. In some industries a due date won't hurt quality, so there is no conflict.

**Jumping to the goal without needs.** Writing the goal first can warp the whole cloud. *Fix:* Work right to left. Actions, then needs, then a goal that summarises both needs.

**The goal is too generic.** A vague goal gives you weak connections to challenge later. *Fix:* The goal must cover both needs, but make it as specific as you reasonably can. "Successful sales department" beats "successful business."

| Step | What you do |
|---|---|
| 1. Actions | Write the two clashing actions (the conflict you feel) |
| 2. Needs | Ask "why?" for each action and write the need behind it |
| 3. Goal | Write the common goal that ties both needs together |
| Read as | "In order to [need], I must [action]" |
