# Core Conflict Injections and the Future Reality Tree (FRT)

## Part 1: Core Conflict Injections

### What It Is

A core conflict injection (CCI) is an injection that breaks the fundamental conflict at the heart of your CRT. It works the same way as resolving a regular conflict cloud: you challenge the assumptions behind the connections between the conflicting pressures and the needs they serve.

The difference is scale and stubbornness. A core conflict is deeply embedded. It's what keeps your subject stable over the long term. You might lean toward one side for weeks, months, or even years, but the goal violations always push you back. Because it's driven by the core needs of the subject, it's almost impossible to ignore or avoid.

This is why fixing individual branches of your CRT rarely leads to lasting change. The core forces derail them eventually. Addressing the core conflict first is what makes significant, durable improvement possible. It doesn't solve the entire CRT on its own, but it enables you to do that in the next step.

### Why It Matters

Small fixes address symptoms. The core conflict is what holds the whole pattern in place. Until it's broken, the same pressures keep generating the same behaviours and the same goal violations.

Once the core conflict is resolved, it reduces certain pressures and the behaviours that flow from them. That creates the conditions for the rest of the CRT to be resolved.

### What You Should Do

The injection process is the same as for a regular cloud, but you need to be more thorough and more creative.

Core conflicts are not held in place by obvious flawed assumptions. If they were, they would have been resolved long ago. The "obvious" injections have usually been tried before with limited results.

**Aim for 10 to 30 assumptions on a single connection.** The first few will be the most logical and well-known. As you push further down the list, you're forced into more creative and less-considered territory. Those later assumptions are often where the most powerful injection ideas come from.

**Don't chase the easiest injection.** Chase the one with the best impact. Good core conflict injections are often difficult: they may require a long-term plan, significant investment, or a meaningful change to how the system operates. Entertain the ambitious ones before writing them off. Most people get attached to the first idea they like and dismiss the more complex ones without fully considering them.

There will always be a cost-to-benefit trade-off, and you may need to work within real constraints. But giving the more creative options a fair chance is what separates a surface-level fix from a genuine breakthrough.

## Part 2: The Future Reality Tree (FRT)

### What It Is

A Future Reality Tree is the CRT transformed by your solution set. You insert your injections into the CRT and use cause-and-effect logic to map out what will change.

The FRT has two purposes:
- **Build a complete solution set.** Breaking the core conflict is necessary but rarely sufficient. The FRT shows where the existing injections leave gaps and where additional ones are needed.
- **Test and verify the logic.** The CRT confirmed you understand the problem. The FRT confirms your solution will actually work. Many solutions sound good but fail under logical scrutiny. The FRT forces you to predict the outcome of each injection step by step.

### What You Should Do

Work through these steps in order.

**1. Insert the core conflict injection.**
Place the CCI into the CRT at the connection it breaks. This is the starting point for the FRT.

**2. Test its impact.**
Trace the effects of the CCI forward through the causal chains. How much does it change? In some subjects it will have a large knock-on effect; in others it may break the conflict but leave many branches largely intact. This is not a measure of how good the injection is. It simply reflects the structure of the subject.

**3. Work up the chains and find the gaps.**
Move up each causal chain from the CCI. Stop at any point where the existing injections are not sufficient to change the outcome. These are the spots where additional injections are needed.

**4. Add injections until the CRT is sufficiently resolved.**
Repeat the process for each gap. Keep going until all the major problems on the CRT are resolved or reduced to an acceptable level.

You don't need to eliminate every undesirable effect. Cutting a defect rate to zero is impractical. Cutting it in half could be a massive improvement. Aim for sufficient, not perfect.

**5. List all injections as your solution set.**
Once you're happy with the FRT, compile all the injections you intend to implement. This is your solution set and the basis for your implementation plan.

## Quick Reference

### Core Conflict Injection

| Step | What you do |
|---|---|
| 1. List assumptions | Aim for 10 to 30 on the key connection |
| 2. Push past the obvious | The early ones are well-known; the later ones spark creative ideas |
| 3. Pick for impact | Not the easiest injection, the one with the best effect |
| 4. Entertain the ambitious | Don't write off complex ideas before considering them fully |

### Future Reality Tree

| Step | What you do |
|---|---|
| 1. Insert the CCI | Place it at the connection it breaks |
| 2. Test the impact | Trace how far the change flows through the chains |
| 3. Find the gaps | Stop where existing injections are not enough |
| 4. Add injections | Fill each gap until the CRT is sufficiently resolved |
| 5. Compile the solution set | List all injections to implement |

| Principle | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Fix the core conflict first | Individual branch fixes rarely last |
| Test with cause-and-effect logic | Solutions that sound good often fail under scrutiny |
| Sufficient, not perfect | A major reduction beats chasing zero |
