# Critical Thinking: Cause-and-Effect, Prerequisites, and Assumptions

## Cause-and-Effect Logic

**What it is**

Cause-and-effect logic (formerly called Sufficiency Logic) looks at how outcomes come to be, or are claimed to come to be. One thing causes another.

Examples:
- If I push the brake pedal, then the car stops.
- If a manager yells at someone, then that person quits.
- If I prepare for a meeting, then the meeting often has a better outcome.
- If I delegate the task, then I won't get the right outcome.

**Why it matters**

You can break any outcome into a chain of links. How much detail you go into depends on your role and your goal.

Example: "If I push the brake pedal, then the car stops" can be broken into the pedal, master cylinder, brake lines, callipers, pads, and tires. A mechanic needs that detail to find a broken link. You may only care that the brakes work.

**What you should do**

Use a diagram. Sticky notes, boxes on a whiteboard, or boxes in software all work. Use **vertical arrows** for cause-and-effect, so you always know what you are looking at.

Steps:
1. Write the cause as a complete, standalone statement. Not "quality" but "I have concerns about quality."
2. Write the effect as its own true standalone statement.
3. Draw the arrow between them.
4. Read the connection out loud to check the logic sounds right.

Each statement must make sense without context. You need statements you can agree are true on their own before you link them.

## Prerequisite Logic

**What it is**

Prerequisite logic (formerly called Necessity Logic) covers things that are required for an outcome, but that do not cause it on their own.

A prerequisite is necessary but not sufficient. Fuelling a plane does not cause it to fly. Neither does starting the engines. But the plane cannot fly without both.

Examples:
- In order to bake a cake, I need eggs.
- In order to join a meeting, I need an internet connection.
- In order to get a promotion, I need to bring the current project in on time.
- In order to use my phone, I must charge the battery.

**Why it matters**

What counts as a prerequisite depends on your goal.

Example: If the goal is just for the plane to fly, passengers are not a prerequisite, but pilots are. If the goal is a commercially successful flight, passengers likely become a prerequisite.

This matters because people often label things as prerequisites when they are really nice-to-haves. Getting this right keeps you flexible.

**What you should do**

Use **horizontal arrows** for prerequisite logic, so it is easy to tell apart from cause-and-effect.

Steps:
1. Start with the outcome as a standalone statement. "Use my phone."
2. Write the prerequisite as a standalone statement. "I must charge my battery."
3. Draw the horizontal arrow between them.
4. Read it out loud, verbatim: "In order to use my phone, I must charge my battery."

Always read these as "In order to... I must..." Read exactly what you wrote, or you are not really testing it.

## Prerequisite Assumptions

**What it is**

The assumption is the logic that explains why a prerequisite is needed. It holds the connection together.

Read it as: "In order to A, I must B because [assumption]."

Example: "In order to use my phone, I must charge it, because phones require batteries to work." The assumption is "phones require batteries to work."

**Why it matters**

Prerequisite assumptions are a bit less robust than cause-and-effect ones. They do not have to tie together everything that could cause the outcome. They only explain why that one thing is a prerequisite.

Assumptions capture what you believe about a situation. That lets you be critical of it, both in your own logic and in what others claim.

People often insert prerequisites that are not truly necessary. They claim things can't move forward until some condition is met, when that is not actually true.

**What you should do**

Use assumptions to challenge prerequisites:
- Why is this a prerequisite, not just a nice-to-have?
- Is this still true today, or just something we have always done?
- Are there other ways to reach the outcome?

Check the logic before you commit to something. Often the assumption that "we need B to get A" is no longer true, and challenging it opens up better options and decisions.

## Quick Reference

| | Cause-and-Effect | Prerequisite |
|---|---|---|
| **Old name** | Sufficiency Logic | Necessity Logic |
| **Core idea** | One thing causes another | One thing is required but does not cause |
| **Arrow direction** | Vertical | Horizontal |
| **Read as** | "If [cause], then [effect]" | "In order to [A], I must [B]" |
| **Assumption** | Ties the full logic together | Explains why B is required for A |
