# The Druid Loop: Understanding Recurring Conflicts

## What It Is

A druid (short for "druid loop") maps a recurring conflict between two behaviours that you keep swinging between.

Here is the pattern. You pick one behaviour. It causes a negative side effect that builds until something feels too wrong (a goal violation). That pushes you to the opposite behaviour. But that behaviour causes its own negative side effect, which builds to its own goal violation, pushing you back to the first. Round and round you go.

This only applies to problems that come up again and again. If a real solution existed, you would have fixed it long ago and the loop would be gone.

A "goal violation" is not always formal. It just means something feels so wrong, to you or someone else, that it drives a switch to the other behaviour.

Example: You go out with friends, drink too much, and have a rough couple of days. That pushes you to stay home next time. But staying home gets boring and you feel like you are missing out. That pushes you back out again. Around and around.

## Why It Matters

When two sides are stuck in a druid, each side is focused on its own pressures and blind to the harm its approach causes the other. You see your side. They see theirs. Neither sees the whole.

Mapping it out changes the fight from "you vs. me" into "us vs. the problem." You stop arguing about who is right and start solving the real issue together.

Benefits:
- **Solve it for good.** Seeing both sides and the cause-and-effect steps lets you fix the root issue instead of slapping on another Band-Aid (which is how you got the loop in the first place).
- **See other perspectives.** Building the habit of mapping the other side helps you find win-win solutions.
- **Get sharper.** Smart problem-solvers tend to see situations clearly and spot real fixes. This tool trains exactly that.

## What You Should Do

### Build the druid (work from the behaviours up)

1. **Find the behaviour pair.** Two mutually exclusive behaviours you keep switching between. If you can't find a pair, you probably don't have a druid. Word them as behaviours (things you actually do), not concepts. "Get involved in quality discussions" is a behaviour. "Quality" is not.

2. **Pick the side you know best and build its cause-and-effect branch.** One side's negatives will come to you more easily. Start there. Write the steps: if I do this behaviour, then this happens, then that happens, up to a goal violation.

3. **Check the goal violation drives the flip.** Make sure that negative is bad enough to push you (or the other party) toward the opposite behaviour.

4. **Build the other side the same way.** This is often the trickier side, your own. Ask: what goes wrong here? What do others complain about? Run the steps up to its goal violation, and check it drives the flip back.

You should end with two mutually exclusive behaviours at the bottom, cause-and-effect steps rising from each, and a goal violation at the top of each that pushes you to the other side.

### Worked example: managing a team

Behaviour pair: "I get involved in my team's work day-to-day" vs. "I leave the team to manage themselves day-to-day."

Left side (the one you know best):
- If I get involved day-to-day, then I'm in a lot of the team's work steps.
- If I'm in a lot of the steps, then the team often waits for me.
- If the team waits for me, then work slows down. **(goal violation)**
- Slow work pushes me to leave the team to manage themselves.

Right side:
- If I leave the team to manage themselves, then they sometimes decide without all the information.
- If they decide without all the information, then work sometimes heads the wrong way.
- If work heads the wrong way, then we get a lot of rework. **(goal violation)**
- Rework pushes me to get involved again.

## How to Check Your Druid

Run these tests after you build it:

- **Behaviours.** Both base entities are actually behaviours, not concepts or outcomes.
- **Mutually exclusive.** You can't do both at once (often due to limited time, money, or people), so you focus on one side at a time. That is what causes the looping.
- **Cause and effect.** Pull any two connected boxes out of context. Does the lower one really cause the upper one? Check each link.
- **True now.** Both sides are actually happening, or have happened, not just a fear of what might go wrong. You may be on one side now, but the other side has happened before and you can feel the loop trending back.
- **Serious goal violations.** Each top entity is a real, strong negative, not a mild "I suppose that's slightly bad." A weak negative won't drive a switch.
- **Loop makes sense.** Each goal violation actually raises the pressure to do the opposite behaviour.

## Common Errors

**Repetition.** Two connected boxes are technically different but practically the same. Example: "If the ice cream slips out of my hand, then I drop the ice cream." True, but it wastes a step. Use steps to bridge real gaps in logic, not to restate the same thing.

**Premature looping.** The goal violation includes the other side's behaviour. You ended the chain with your response to the negative instead of the negative itself. Example: discounting leads to lower profit, which pushes you to "sell at full price." But "sell at full price" is the other behaviour and belongs only on the right side. Stop at the negative.

**Failed solution.** One side is the problem behaviour and the other is a "solution" whose only negative is that it doesn't work. When the solution fails, you default straight back to the problem, so both sides become the same druid. A druid needs two real competing approaches, each with its own distinct negative. Example: "build buy-in" vs. "leave people to do their own thing" fails if the only downside of building buy-in is that it simply doesn't work.

**Tangent (off-topic goal violation).** Step-by-step building drifts to a different theme, so the final goal violation no longer matches the base behaviour. Example: time pressure was meant to lead to quality faults, but you drifted into staff complaints instead. Compare the top and base entities to make sure they still tell the story you intended.

**Stopping short.** You ended before the real negative that actually triggers the switch. Example: shortcuts cause quality defects, but defects alone may not change anyone's behaviour. It might take a defect hitting a valued customer before someone acts. So "quality defects" may be the wrong goal violation.

## Quick Reference

| Step | What you do |
|---|---|
| 1. Behaviour pair | Two mutually exclusive behaviours you swing between |
| 2. First branch | Build cause-and-effect steps from the side you know best |
| 3. Goal violation | End on a strong negative that drives the flip |
| 4. Second branch | Build the other side up to its own goal violation |
| Read steps as | "If [this], then [that]" |
| Core idea | Two behaviours, each causing a negative that drives the other |
| Goal | Turn "you vs. me" into "us vs. the problem" |
