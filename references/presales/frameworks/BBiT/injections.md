# Injections: Solving Problems by Challenging Assumptions

## What It Is

An injection is a state or fact that, if it were true right now, would change your situation and remove your problem.

Big companies did this. Tesla challenged the assumption that an electric car is just an efficient eco-platform and made it a high-performance machine. Apple challenged what a phone could be. Netflix challenged the idea that movies must be rented one at a time. Each one asked: "Does it have to be that way?"

Your injections don't need to be world-changing. "I don't need coffee to function" or "that's not the only way we can do it here" are injections too.

Two rules for a good injection:
- It solves the problem.
- It is **not an action**. Word it as a state or a done statement ("the cat has access to food"), not a task ("ask the neighbour to feed the cat").

## Why It Matters

You can't list ideas that would never occur to you. As the book *Farsighted* puts it, no amount of analysis lets a person write down things they would never think of.

The injection process gets around this. You list all the things that **do** occur to you (your assumptions), then flip them on their heads. Most of the flips will be useless or nonsense. That's fine. You only need **one** that makes you say "yes, we could make that happen."

Why word it as a state, not an action? Because the state is your target. The first action you try might not work, and different people or departments may need different actions to hit the same target. Naming the state keeps everyone aiming at the same goal.

Example: "A neighbour feeds the cat" is an action. It breaks if the neighbour moves or goes away too. "The cat has access to food" is the state. Many actions can deliver it.

## What You Should Do

It is a two-step process: raise assumptions, then reverse them into injections.

### Step 1: Raise assumptions

List every assumption behind your connection. Use the wording that fits:
- Cause and effect: "If this, then that, because..."
- Prerequisite: "In order to X, I must Y, because..."

**Do not filter.** Writing down "bad" ones is the hardest part, and the most important. Two reasons:
- A throwaway assumption often triggers a great injection once reversed.
- Unwritten ideas clog your head and slow you down.

There is no downside to 20 ideas where 10 are rubbish. Trying to write only the good ones usually gets you 2, then you stall.

### Step 2: Reverse into injections

Flip each assumption. The opposite of "white" doesn't have to be "black," just "not white." Pick the wording that is most useful.

- You can get more than one injection from a single assumption.
- It is easiest to edit your existing list (cross out, or copy and edit) rather than rewrite everything.

Then pick the one injection you like best and that seems reasonable. Test it: add it back like a future assumption and ask, "If this were true, would it break the connection?"

Worry about the actions to make it true **later**. Right now, you just need the target.

### Worked example (cause and effect)

Connection: "If we raise our prices, then we will lose customers."

Assumptions:
- Customers only care about price.
- Customers are close to leaving anyway.
- We don't have a strong value proposition.
- We compete heavily on price.
- There is no reason to stay at a higher price.

Reversed into injections:
- Customers care about more than price.
- Customers aren't close to leaving.
- We have a strong value proposition.
- We don't compete heavily on price.
- **There are reasons to stay with us at a higher price.**

Pick the last one. If there are reasons to stay at a higher price, raising prices won't lose customers. It works. (Action ideas later: loyalty perks, lounge access, frequent flyer programs.)

## Applying It to the Bigger Tools

For prerequisite connections, clouds, and druids, the core process is the same. You just add two steps: **pick which connection to solve**, and at the end **check the whole tool is resolved**, not just one isolated piece.

### Prerequisite connection

Same process. Example: "In order to have a successful meeting, we must involve the whole team." One injection: "We would be more likely to achieve the goal without everyone present." If true, the whole team is no longer necessary, so the connection breaks. (Action ideas later: fewer people, staggered meetings, smaller focused groups.)

### Clouds

A cloud has four prerequisite connections plus the conflict between the two actions.

- Leave the action-vs-action conflict aside. If it needs solving, it is usually the hardest part.
- Pick one of the four prerequisite connections, usually one of the two with an action on one end.
- When working with others, break **your own** side. Breaking their side makes people defensive, like you are pulling a trick. You still get the effect you want.
- Run the assumption-and-injection process, then check the injection resolves the **whole cloud**.

Example: "In order to ensure the task is done well, I must do it myself." Injection picked: "Others can do the task well enough." If true, the conflict between doing it yourself and handing it over disappears, and the cloud resolves.

### Druids

Pick which of the many connections to solve. Two things to weigh:
- **Where** can you resolve it before things get really bad?
- **What** looks easy or shaky enough to break? Some links are tight; others already look loose.

Then run the process. Example connection: "If some of my work doesn't get done, then work starts to pile up." Injection picked: "The customers are going to do the work themselves." If true, work doesn't pile up. (Think beta testing or customer self-service.)

Finally, check the injection flows around the **whole druid** and breaks the recurring loop, not just the one connection.

## Quick Reference

| Step | What you do |
|---|---|
| 1. Raise assumptions | List every assumption behind the connection. Don't filter. |
| 2. Reverse | Flip each into an injection (a state, not an action) |
| 3. Pick | Choose the one that seems reasonable and would solve it |
| 4. Test | Add it back as a future assumption: does it break the connection? |
| Extra for clouds/druids | First pick which connection; at the end check the whole tool is resolved |
| Golden rule | An injection is a state, never an action. Sort out actions later. |
