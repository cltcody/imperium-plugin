# Negative Branch Reservations (NBR): Handling the Downsides of a Good Idea

## What It Is

A Negative Branch Reservation (NBR) is a tool for dealing with the negative side effects of an idea you want to pursue.

Even great ideas can have downsides. We call them side effects because the main effect is the positive one. But the downsides can derail a good solution, because you (or others) hesitate to act.

The name breaks down like this:
- **Negative:** the bad side effect you are worried about.
- **Branch:** you map it with cause-and-effect logic that branches off your main positive idea.
- **Reservation:** the concern that holds you back from proceeding.

Side effects come in two types:
- **Legitimate:** a real downside you must mitigate or accept. (A $5k cost for a $100k benefit? Just wear it.)
- **Fear:** a worry that, once mapped, turns out not to actually happen.

The tool helps you tell the two apart, and trim the branch when the negative is real.

Examples: medication side effects, or getting a dog (sleepless puppy nights, can't go out for long, it might growl at housemates). Plan for the downsides and the main idea goes ahead smoothly.

### How an NBR differs from a normal causal chain

An NBR **starts with an action or injection you actually want to do**, something with a good reason behind it. It can even be unpleasant (a run), as long as it leads to an outcome you want. A normal causal chain might start with a pure negative you'd never choose ("if I break my leg, then I'm in pain"). You don't choose to break your leg.

## Why It Matters

The success of a great idea often depends on how you handle its downsides. Without this tool, you tend to either hand-wave away real risks because you like the idea, or kill a great idea over a fear that was never going to happen.

NBR situations are everywhere. When the negative is small or delayed ("that's a problem for future me"), you act anyway. When it gets serious, you delay or avoid something that would actually help you. Mapping it gives you foresight: strong contingency plans, and no wasted effort on "what ifs."

## What You Should Do

The five steps spell out **N-B-R**: Negative effect, Branch, Resolve.

### Step 1: Negative effect

Identify the action or injection (the cause) and the significant negative outcome you're worried about (the effect). Put the cause at the bottom, the negative at the top. If a worry isn't significant, it doesn't belong in an NBR.

### Step 2: Branch

Connect the two with cause-and-effect steps. Aim for at least four steps (like a druid). Adding assumptions as you go keeps it clear.

If linking the cause to the negative is very hard, that is a sign you're worrying about something unlikely. The NBR may resolve itself right here.

You can have more steps, and branches that split (one cause leading to two different negatives).

### Step 3 & 4: Resolve

Resolve in one of two ways.

**First, is it legitimate or just a fear?** Check the logic and the assumptions. Watch the **magnitude**: a small thing going slightly wrong doesn't mean a huge blowout. If an assumption doesn't hold ("customers have no tolerance for defects" when you know minor typos won't lose anyone), the branch is broken. It was a fear.

**If it's legitimate, either live with it or inject.** First ask if you can simply accept it given the positives. If not, grade each step **positive, neutral, or negative**:
- The starting cause is positive (you want it).
- The top is negative.
- The steps run from positive through neutral to negative.

Place your injection **after all the positives but before all the negatives**, so you keep the upside and trim the downside. Neutrals give you flexibility.

### Step 5: Inject

Use the normal injection process on the chosen connection: raise assumptions, reverse them into injections, pick one, and check it removes the negative. It doesn't need to turn the negative into a positive. It only needs to remove the negative so you can proceed.

### Worked example

Injection: delegate more work to a team member. Two worries: customers leave, and the team spends less time on revenue activity.

- **"Customers leave"** branch relies on "customers have no tolerance for defects." That's a big jump. Minor defects won't lose customers; only major ones would, and a competent team won't suddenly produce those. **This one is a fear. Resolved.**
- **"Less time on revenue activity"** through rework is **legitimate**. Inject: "the company has spare capacity in other teams." That spare capacity covers rework or revenue work. **Resolved.**

## NBR vs. Druid

People often think an NBR is just half a druid. There's overlap, but they fit different situations.

- **Druid:** existing, recurring problems where both sides actually happen and loop back, each pressuring you toward the other.
- **NBR:** predicting, avoiding, or resolving a negative. Often a **one-off**, where the action or negative happens once and doesn't loop.

Two common mismatches:
- Building a druid where one side has **never actually happened** (it's really an NBR). This signals bias toward a preconceived solution instead of objective analysis.
- Building a druid for a **one-off decision** just because your thinking goes back and forth. Getting a dog feels like a loop, but you don't un-get and re-get the dog. An NBR or cloud fits better.

Both tools are cause-and-effect chains analysing how a choice leads to a negative, so the "wrong" tool can still help. The point is to pick the most useful thought pattern.

## Extra Considerations

**Contingencies.** Sometimes a negative is unlikely now but would become likely if the environment changed. Add that change as a new cause in the branch, like a "negative injection." The NBR becomes a contingency plan you act on only if the condition arrives. Decide how far to go; over-planning for "what ifs" wastes time.

**Multiple paths.** A negative can happen in more than one way. Resolve each chain separately. Factor in your risk tolerance. Driving a car has many risks; we mitigate some (seat belts, speed limits, airbags) and accept others because the cost of mitigation is too high. How far you go is a personal call.

## Common Errors

**Positive outcomes in the NBR.** Don't try to prove the idea is good by adding its benefits into the branch. Assess benefits *before* the NBR. The NBR is only for the unwanted side effects of an action you already want. You don't use an NBR to decide whether painkillers cure your headache; you use it to handle the nausea they might cause.

**Biased causality.** Letting bias inflate or shrink the risk:
- Love the action → you downplay the negative.
- Dislike the action → you overplay it.
- You personally bear the downside while others get the upside → it feels worse than it is.

Keep the logic objective. Don't force a weak link or misrepresent facts to make the negative look more or less likely than it is.

## Quick Reference

| Step | What you do |
|---|---|
| N. Negative effect | Cause at the bottom, significant negative at the top |
| B. Branch | Link them with cause-and-effect steps (aim for 4+) |
| R. Resolve: fear? | Check logic, assumptions, and magnitude. If it doesn't hold, it's a fear |
| R. Resolve: live with it? | Accept the negative if the positives outweigh it |
| R. Resolve: inject | Grade steps +/neutral/-, place injection after positives, before negatives |
| Core rule | NBR starts with something you *want* to do; it targets the side effects only |
| Tool choice | Recurring + loops back = druid. One-off negative = NBR |
