# Prerequisite Analysis (PRT): Turning Obstacles Into a Plan

## What It Is

Prerequisite analysis is a tool for getting unstuck. You use it when you already have a goal, solution, or injection, but you don't know how to make it real.

The idea: list what is blocking you, then flip each obstacle into its "overcome" state. That overcome state is an **intermediate objective (IO)**, a milestone on the way to your goal.

Marcus Aurelius captured it: "What stands in the way becomes the way." Once you name what blocks you, you have also named what you need to do to move past it. The obstacle becomes the path.

Example: a flat tyre and you've never changed one. Instead of panicking, list why you can't:
- I don't know where to put the jack.
- I don't know if I have a spare or how to get it out.
- I don't know if I can loosen the nuts.
- I don't know how to make sure the new wheel is secure.

Flip each into a done state: car is jacked up, spare is on the ground, flat wheel is off, new wheel is on. Done = the car is mobile again. Now the easy actions are obvious: call a friend, watch a YouTube video, Google your car model.

## Why It Matters

Great ideas often die at the "how do we actually do this?" stage. They feel too hard, get put aside, and all that effort never sees daylight.

This tool turns "this is overwhelming" into "I have a clear plan" in about half an hour. It works alone or in a meeting, and it lets you align a team around a shared set of objectives.

It pairs with the druid. The druid is your day-to-day tool for understanding and solving problems you don't yet have an answer to. Prerequisite analysis is your day-to-day tool for when you already have the answer but you're stuck.

## What You Should Do

Three steps: list obstacles, turn them into intermediate objectives, then sequence them into a plan.

### Step 1: Set your goal and list obstacles

Start with your goal. If it came from a druid, your injection, worded exactly as it is, becomes your goal. Since an injection doesn't exist yet, ask: why not? What is blocking it from being true now?

List **five to ten** obstacles.
- Fewer than five usually means you haven't really thought about it, or the task is simple enough that you don't need this process.
- More than ten often means several obstacles cluster around one point. Break that out into its own separate (nested) plan.

### Step 2: Turn obstacles into intermediate objectives

For each obstacle, ask: what would it look like if this were overcome? Write that as the IO.

Two rules:
- **Write IOs as done states, not actions.** "The car has enough gas," not "Walk to the gas station." A done state is something you can test ("are we there yet?") and reach through many possible actions. If you write an action and it fails (your friend doesn't pick up), you're stuck again.
- **Make them sufficient, not over-sufficient.** Just enough to clear the obstacle. "We have the resources needed for a successful launch," not "we have unlimited time and money." You list obstacles only because they block you, so you only need enough to unblock.

Not every IO is a direct reversal. "I don't have the materials" becomes "I have access to the materials I need," which leaves room for many actions to get there.

### Step 3: Sequence into a plan (work right to left)

Your IOs are rarely a simple top-to-bottom list. Sequence them by prerequisite.

1. Put the **goal at the end** (the right). Everything else comes before it.
2. Take the first IO and place it on the plan.
3. For each next IO, decide one of three things: does it go **before** another, **after** another, or is it a **separate chain** entirely?
4. Read each link back as "In order to [later], we must [earlier]" to check the logic.

You plan right to left, but you **execute left to right**, starting with the IOs that have no prerequisites.

Tip: keep the obstacle written next to each IO. Before pouring effort into a later IO, check the obstacle still exists. Action on earlier IOs often clears later ones, so the plan tends to collapse near the end.

You can **nest** plans. If one IO is a big goal in itself, the person it's delegated to can run this whole process again to build their own mini-plan.

## Common Errors

**Too many "I don't knows."** Not knowing is a valid obstacle, but a list full of nothing else means your thinking got too narrow. Ask: "If I knew everything I currently don't, could I reach the goal with no difficulty?" The answer is usually no, so dig for the other obstacles too.

**Insufficient obstacles.** Listing only a couple of big obstacles and hand-waving the rest. This happens when you feel confident on the topic. It gives a false sense of security and leaves you (or your team) surprised during execution. A thorough list is little extra work.

**Over-sufficient IOs.** An IO far bigger than needed. If you're the worst player on the team, "I am the best player" overcomes it but is hard and slow. "I am not the worst player," or "I have fun regardless," clears the obstacle and is far more achievable.

**Chasing the IO.** The obstacle can clear before the IO is fully met, yet you keep pushing. The IO is only a means to an end. If you now enjoy the sport, your skill is no longer an obstacle, even if you're still technically the worst player. Stop when the obstacle is gone.

## Quick Reference

| Step | What you do |
|---|---|
| 1. Goal + obstacles | State the goal, list 5 to 10 things blocking you |
| 2. Intermediate objectives | Flip each obstacle into a done state, sufficient not over-sufficient |
| 3. Sequence | Goal at the end, order IOs by prerequisite, read links back |
| Plan vs. execute | Plan right to left; execute left to right |
| Golden rule | IOs are done states, never actions |
| Handy check | Keep the obstacle beside each IO; skip the IO if the obstacle is gone |
