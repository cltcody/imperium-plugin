# Reality Engineering: Choosing a Subject and Applying Scrutiny

## Part 1: Choosing a Subject

### What It Is

A subject is the topic you will analyse and improve. It is a name or definition for an ongoing area of your life or work, not a one-off goal.

Examples:
- Generic: "my job," "my health"
- More specific: "managing my team," "my fitness"

Either width is fine. It depends how broad or narrow you want your analysis to be.

A subject contains **multiple problems or issues**, not just one. Where a druid captures a single recurring problem, a subject can have several druids operating at once. The purpose is to understand how all those issues relate and find the core problem driving the subject.

### What a Subject Is Not

A subject is not a goal. This is the most common mistake.

Goals have obstacles to overcome and milestones to hit, which suit the prerequisite tool. They often have decisions to make, which suit the cloud. A subject is stable and ongoing. It recurs over time. It has persistent behaviours and undesirable effects without an easy fix.

A good rule of thumb: has this thing happened before, rather than being something new you're trying to do? If you find yourself writing a long list of obstacles at the start, that's a red flag. You probably have a goal, not a subject.

| Goal | Subject |
|---|---|
| Lose 20lbs by summer | My weight / health |
| Build a new software feature | Our development process |
| Complete project X by year end | Managing projects in our business |
| Finish in the top 5% of my class | My approach to university |

The good news: analysing the subject still helps you reach your goal. And it helps with every future version of that goal too, because you change the underlying system, not just hit one target.

### Four Criteria for a Good Subject

**Intuition.** You need real knowledge and experience. If you're guessing to fill gaps, the analysis loses value and you lose momentum. Minimum six months in the subject area, preferably one to two years.

**Care.** You need to be invested. If you don't care, you'll oversimplify, take shortcuts, or give up when it gets hard. Work-related subjects are usually a strong starting point.

**Size.** Not too small, not too big. A single druid is too small. Your whole business or "my life" is too big. Aim for a subject with **three to five distinct issues** you want to focus on.

**Influence.** You need to be able to cause change. If you're powerless to act, the analysis becomes detached and either cynical or silly. You don't need to control everything, but you do need meaningful influence.

### Other Things to Consider

**Avoid highly emotional subjects.** The process works on them, but the pace and depth required can be overwhelming and difficult to share with others. Topics like a failed marriage, parenting success, or strongly held political or spiritual beliefs are best tackled on your own time once you have more practice.

**Work subjects are usually best** if your employer is funding or supporting the course. A breakthrough on your shoe collection won't delight your manager.

**Keep a consistent perspective.** Analyse from one point of view throughout. If it's a work subject, stay at the business level. "We deliver late" fits. "I feel like I'm doing a bad job" mixes in a personal perspective and muddies the analysis. "Staff feel like they are doing a bad job" can be valid if it matters to the business, since it stays in the business frame.

## Part 2: Scrutiny

### What It Is

Scrutiny is the process of checking cause-and-effect logic for errors, in the same way proofreading checks for spelling and grammar mistakes. Instead of grammar, you are looking for errors in logic.

It has two goals:
- **Clarity:** is this clearly and accurately expressed?
- **Accuracy:** is this actually true and correct?

The basic version taught here focuses on clarity. Accuracy is covered in the advanced module.

### Why It Matters

Flawed logic is common. It comes from poor communication, incorrect beliefs, bias, or spin, whether deliberate or innocent. Acting on flawed logic wastes time, money, and effort, and can lead to real harm.

More specifically, scrutiny:
- Forces you to understand the problem properly, not just assume you do. Writing things clearly exposes hidden assumptions and logic gaps.
- Allows others to contribute meaningfully. If only you "get what you mean," others can't help.
- Creates better solutions. You can't reliably fix something you only half understand.

Scrutiny is also connected to the Theory of Constraints tradition of checking logical trees using **Categories of Legitimate Reservation (CLRs)**: a structured set of questions for testing whether cause-and-effect connections are valid and well-formed. The clarity reservation is the first and most accessible of these.

### What You Should Do

When reviewing a logical connection (cause-and-effect or prerequisite), check two things:

#### 1. Clarity on the entity

Is each statement clear on its own? Does it make sense as a sentence? Are there words that are ambiguous, jargon-heavy, or open to multiple interpretations?

If not, flag it and rework the entity so it accurately reflects what you mean.

#### 2. Clarity on the connection

Do the entities make sense together? Even if each statement looks fine individually, does the cause actually lead to the effect? Can you see how one produces the other?

If not, flag it. Usually the fix is to change the entities, because unclear connections typically mean the entities have wrong or missing information.

#### How to apply it

Mark issues with a token (a simple flag or note) rather than stopping to fix them immediately. Review first, then edit. Separating the two steps keeps you focused.

You can also be more specific. If one word is the issue, ask for clarity on just that word. For example, "Clarity on 'people'" where the entity uses "people" when it clearly only applies to customers.

#### How much scrutiny to apply

Match the level to the stakes. High-value, high-cost decisions deserve more rigorous scrutiny. Lower-stakes analysis can be more relaxed. Technical terms are fine if the audience understands them and they're not being used to create ambiguity.

While learning, apply scrutiny a little more strictly than you normally would. It builds the habit and makes informal scrutiny in everyday life much easier.

#### How many flags to use

Aim for **three to five tokens per side of a druid** as a starting point. This is enough to drive meaningful improvement without overwhelming the author. Fix the big issues first; smaller ones often resolve themselves as the main ones are addressed.

Work iteratively. Multiple short rounds of scrutiny and revision produce better results than trying to perfect everything in one go.

## Quick Reference

### Choosing a Subject

| Check | Question |
|---|---|
| Intuition | Have you spent at least 6 months in this area? |
| Care | Are you invested enough to finish a full analysis? |
| Size | Does it have 3 to 5 distinct issues? |
| Influence | Can you actually cause change here? |
| Subject vs. goal | Has this thing happened before? Or is it something new? |

### Scrutiny

| Type | What to check |
|---|---|
| Clarity on the entity | Is each statement clear, unambiguous, and readable on its own? |
| Clarity on the connection | Does the cause actually lead to the effect as written? |

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|
| Review before editing | Flag first, then fix |
| Be specific | Flag the exact word or phrase if possible |
| Keep it manageable | 3 to 5 tokens per druid side to start |
| Work iteratively | Multiple rounds beat one perfect pass |
