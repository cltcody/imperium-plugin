# Transition Logic (TrT): Tying Actions to the Outcomes You Want

## What It Is

Transition logic is a tool that helps you get the outcome you want more reliably and more often. You build a simple table with three columns:

- **Effect:** the outcome you need. What does "done" look like?
- **Action:** what you (or someone) will do to get there. This is a suggestion, and can change.
- **Logic:** why you believe the action will deliver the effect.

By default we think in actions. We focus on what to do, and only notice a problem when someone does something and it doesn't lead to the result we wanted. Transition logic flips the focus back to the outcome, while still planning the "how."

## Why It Matters

**It keeps actions aligned with the goal.** You stop wasting time, money, and effort on things that serve no purpose. This matters most when delegating. Someone can do exactly what you asked, but if it doesn't deliver the outcome, it was wasted. Naming the effect first gives people a target and makes success more likely.

**It creates freedom and flexibility.** When you fixate on one way of doing things, you miss alternatives. Stepping back to the outcome opens up other ideas.

**The logic column sense-checks your thinking.** You often prefer an action just because it's how you've always done it, or from a hidden assumption. Writing the logic forces you to ask, consciously, whether it's really the best approach.

**The logic column transfers understanding.** Telling someone exactly how to do something only teaches them to repeat a process. Giving the logic helps them understand the situation, so they can course-correct and spot problems. Most "stupid" mistakes are just people doing what they were told without the context to choose better.

## What You Should Do

For each change, work in this order:

1. **Find the reason for the change.** If there's no reason, why change at all?
2. **Get clear on the effect.** When it's done, what will the new state look like? How will you know you succeeded?
3. **Choose an action.** The best approach you can think of, treated as a suggestion that can be modified.
4. **Write the logic.** Why will this action deliver this effect?

Make sure the three columns are **distinct**. Each should add unique information. "If we need a plan, make a plan, because making a plan gets you a plan" is true but useless. Better: the effect is "everyone knows what they need to do and in what order," the action is "make a plan," and the logic is "plans determine and communicate tasks and set timelines."

## Using It for Forward Planning

A common misconception is that this tool is only for things you already know well (like writing an SOP or delegating a process). It is just as useful, often more so, for things you've never done before.

Why it helps with the unfamiliar:
- **Effects are easier to define than actions.** When tackling something new, it's easier to work out what needs to happen than exactly what to do. Set out the major outcomes first.
- **It gives structure to choosing actions.** Each action is picked because an effect needs it, so you avoid pointless steps.
- **It forces you to scrutinise your actions.** The most common everyday mistake is taking an action that was never going to work. It's easy to spot in others ("that's never going to work") but hard to catch in yourself. Often the idea is right but undercooked, not strong enough to cause real change. The logic column exposes that.

Don't aim for perfect. With anything new there will be flawed assumptions and unknowns. Some actions won't pan out and extra effects will emerge. Don't reject the approach just because it isn't flawless (the perfectionist's fallacy). Your first attempt will be much closer than winging it, and far easier to adjust as you go.

## Common Errors

**Insufficient actions.** Suggesting an action too weak to deliver the effect. It looks fine at a glance but doesn't hold up. Example: you need staff to agree to relocate to another country. A promotional video might raise interest a little, but no one moves to an undesirable country over a video. The logic column forces you to ask whether the action is really enough.

**Effects written as completed actions.** Wording the effect as just "the action is done" instead of the outcome you actually wanted. Example: action "give someone a gift," effect "person has received the gift." That only restates the action. The real effect is "the person's mood has improved." This is harder to spot in complex situations, and usually happens when someone is attached to a specific action and quietly reframes "done" around it.

## Quick Reference

| Column | What it captures |
|---|---|
| Effect | The outcome you need. What does "done" look like? |
| Action | The suggested thing to do (can be changed) |
| Logic | Why the action will deliver the effect |

| Principle | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Outcome first | Keeps actions aligned, avoids wasted effort |
| Action is a suggestion | Leaves freedom and flexibility |
| Always write the logic | Sense-checks your thinking and transfers understanding |
| Don't chase perfect | A rough TrT beats winging it and is easy to adjust |
