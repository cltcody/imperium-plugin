# Theory of Constraints (TOC) + Black Belt in Thinking (BBiT)

> Use TOC to find and improve the true constraint. Use BBiT to challenge assumptions, resolve conflicts, and design solutions that work end to end. By combining both, you stop firefighting, align everyone on the same goal, and create steady, measurable improvement in profit, flow, and quality of life at work.

---

## Five Focusing Steps — Core of Constraint Management

### Step 1 — Identify
Find the system's constraint. It can be physical, policy, paradigm, mindset, or market.

### Step 2 — Exploit (Quick Win)
Make the most of what you already have. Fix what is slowing the constraint without spending money yet.

Examples: reduce downtime, prioritise work for the constraint, add quality checks before it.

### Step 3 — Subordinate
Align everything else to support the constraint. Non-constraints should never starve or block the constraint.

### Step 4 — Elevate
If the constraint remains after exploiting and subordinating, take bigger actions — including investment.

Only invest after Exploit and Subordinate are fully applied.

### Step 5 — Repeat
Once the constraint moves, start again. Never optimise everything at once. Always optimise the constraint. Avoid inertia.

## TOC + BBiT Step-by-Step Process

---

### Step 1 — Identify the Undesirable Effects (UDEs) / Problems

A UDE is a clear statement of something that is not working. A symptom you can observe.

UDEs are the starting point. They help you see problems objectively and later identify the root cause in the Current Reality Tree.

**Use these 4 rules when writing UDEs:**

**Describe only what you see**
No explanations, no opinions.
Example: "Demos often start late."

**Make it standalone and specific**
Anyone should understand it.
Example: "Average deal cycle increased from 60 to 120 days."

**Keep it neutral**
No blame, no emotion.
Example: "We rework proposals in 30 percent of cases."

**Describe the effect, not the cause**
Wrong: "We have long sales cycles because qualification is weak."
Right: "50 percent of opportunities enter discovery without qualification."

**How to collect UDEs:**
Ask: What frustrates you? What keeps repeating? What slows customers down? What data shows a drop in performance? Where do handovers or communication regularly break?

---

### Step 2 — Build the Current Reality Tree (CRT)

**Purpose:** Find what to change.

Link UDEs using if-then causality. The CRT is complete when most UDEs link back to one or a few core drivers.

This answers **TOC Q1: What to change?**

**Three validation tests:**
- **Causality test** — Does the cause reasonably create the effect?
- **Assumption test** — What hidden belief makes this link true?
- **Entity test** — Are the statements themselves clear and valid?

---

### Step 3 — Reveal the Conflict (Cloud)

Find the real conflict behind the symptoms and uncover the needs and assumptions driving people's actions.

**A Cloud appears when:**
- Two actions compete
- Both actions make sense
- Both actions serve important needs
- You cannot choose both at the same time

**How to build the Cloud:**
1. Write the two competing actions (wants). Word them neutrally, without hidden solutions.
2. For each action, write the need it serves. Ask: "Why is this action important?"
3. Define the common goal — the goal that both needs support.
4. List the assumptions. Identify the beliefs that link action to need, and need to goal.
5. Create injections: break or dissolve the assumptions to make both needs achievable.

**Define the conflict clearly.** Ensure the two actions truly conflict and each is tied to a clear need. Avoid naming a false conflict, using unclear needs, or phrasing actions as beliefs instead of concrete wants.

---

### Step 4 — Break the Assumptions (Druids)

You define the new state by breaking the assumptions that create the conflict.

This answers **TOC Q2: What to change to?**

**Steps:**
- List assumptions that link actions to needs. Write each as a clear, testable sentence.
- List assumptions that link needs to mutual benefit. These explain why each need protects the goal.
- Challenge assumptions using logic tests: Is this always true? Does evidence support it? Is it a belief or a fact? Is it required for the link to hold?
- Create multiple injections. Each injection should break or remove an assumption. Do not stop at the first one.
- Select the best injection — the simplest and most practical one that dissolves the conflict.

**Check for win-win:**
- Both needs remain satisfied
- The conflict disappears
- No new negative effects are created

**An injection** is a simple, practical change that breaks the assumptions holding a conflict or problem in place. Think of it as: a smart change that makes the problem disappear.

**Use Druids for recurring or oscillating conflicts.** Druids expose negative loops that pull you back into the same problem. Break loops with injections targeted at violated needs.

---

### Step 5 — Build the Future Reality Tree (FRT)

A tool that shows how your solution will work in the future and checks if it creates any new problems.

This answers **TOC Q3: How to cause the change safely?**

It ensures your solution works in reality, not just in theory.

**Steps:**
1. Place the injections at the bottom of the tree. These are the changes you want to introduce.
2. Build upward using if-then logic. For each injection, ask: if we apply this injection, then what good effect happens? Link effects to other effects until you reach the high-level goal.
3. Validate each link using: causality test, assumption test, sufficiency test.
4. Check for completeness. All injections should naturally lead to the final desired outcome.
5. Identify Negative Branch Reservations (NBRs). Ask: what could go wrong? What negative side effects might appear?
6. Add preventative injections. For each NBR, add a small targeted injection to neutralise the risk.
7. Confirm the win-win state. The FRT must show that all conflicts are dissolved, both needs are satisfied, the system improves without new UDEs, and the final goal is achieved.

**Build the FRT with care.** Validate each proposed cause-effect step using logic tests. Actively look for possible negative side effects ("negative branches") so you can address them in advance.

---

### Step 6 — Plan via the Prerequisite Tree (PRT)

The PRT must start with one clear goal: the future state you want after the injections work.

Write the goal first, before identifying obstacles.

**An obstacle** is a statement of what prevents you from reaching the goal — not a missing action.

Wrong: "We haven't configured the system."
Right: "The system is not configured."

**Intermediate Objectives (IOs)** must be formulated as: "The obstacle is no longer present because..."

**Validate the logic using:**
- **Necessity test** — Does this IO need to exist for the next IO to happen?
- **Assumption test** — What assumptions make this dependency true? Are they valid?
- **Entity test** — Are obstacles and IOs written as clear, standalone statements?

---

### Step 7 — Execute via the Transition Tree (TT)

The TT turns the PRT into executable steps. It shows exactly what to do, why it works, and how each action moves the system forward.

This answers **TOC Q3: How to cause the change?**

**Five elements of each TT step:**

1. **Current State (CS)** — Describe the real situation right now.
   Example: "Requirements are still unclear."

2. **Action (A)** — The concrete step you take next. Must be tied to an Intermediate Objective.
   Example: "Hold a structured clarification workshop with the customer."

3. **Expected Effect (EE)** — What you believe will happen after the action.
   Example: "Requirements become clarified."

4. **Logical Reason (R)** — Explain why this action leads to the effect.
   Example: "Clarification removes ambiguity and aligns expectations."

5. **Actual Effect (AE)** — Check whether the expected effect actually happened. If not, adjust the next action.
   Example: "Requirements clarified and documented."

Use the new state as the starting point for the next CS — A — EE — R — AE chain. Continue until the final goal is reached.

**Use the Transition Tree to plan realistic, step-by-step changes.** Ensure each action is feasible from the current state. Never omit the "why." Every step needs a logical explanation so you know how it moves you toward the goal.

When stuck, return to the CRT or Cloud to ensure you are addressing the real root cause or conflict.

## The Three TOC Questions

| Question | Steps |
|---|---|
| Q1 — What to change? | Steps 1 and 2: UDEs, CRT |
| Q2 — What to change to? | Steps 3 and 4: Cloud and Injections |
| Q3 — How to cause the change? | Steps 5, 6, and 7: FRT, PRT, TT |
