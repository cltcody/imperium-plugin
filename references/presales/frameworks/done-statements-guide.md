# Done Statements: A Practical Guide

A Done Statement describes the **outcome** you want, not the **actions** you take to get there.

This guide explains what they are, why they matter, how to write them, and what to watch out for.

---

## What it is

A Done Statement is a clear sentence that describes the end state you want.

It answers one question: *What does "finished" actually look like?*

- It focuses on the **effect**, not the **task**.
- It is written in the present tense, as if the result already exists.
- It can be checked by someone outside your team.

**Quick example**

- Action-focused: "Develop a user login feature."
- Outcome-focused: "Users can securely and quickly log in to their accounts."

The second one tells you when you are done. The first one only tells you what to start.

---

## Why it matters

When you only describe actions, problems show up fast:

- **You feel unsure how to proceed.** You keep asking for instructions or reassurance.
- **You waste time debating the "how."** Meanwhile the real goal gets ignored.
- **You make poor choices.** You aim for the right actions instead of the right results, so you get rework.
- **People fall out of alignment.** Unclear goals lead to frustration and stalled progress.

Done Statements fix this. They give you:

- **Clarity.** Everyone knows what success looks like.
- **Alignment.** Teams pull in the same direction.
- **Focus.** Effort goes to what matters.
- **Less rework.** You spot wrong paths early.
- **Freedom.** People pick the best method themselves.

There is a wider lesson here from product and project work. Teams often deliver everything they planned, close the project, and still cannot say if anything improved. Shipping work is not the same as creating value. Delivery confirms the work is finished. It does not confirm that something got better. Done Statements keep your eye on the change, not just the delivery.

---

## What you should do

### The 3 key principles

**1. Outcome over Action** *(non-negotiable)*

Describe the result, not the steps.

- Weak: "Develop a user login feature."
- Strong: "Users can securely and quickly log in to their accounts."

This is the only principle you must always use. The other two depend on how much detail the situation needs.

**2. Clarity of purpose**

Make the "why" obvious.

- Weak: "The schedule of upcoming feature releases is published."
- Strong: "Our users are aware of the features being released this quarter."

**3. Objectively verifiable**

Someone outside the team should be able to confirm it. Make it **measurable** or **binary**.

- Measurable: "Our new feature is used by 30% of our customers within six months."
- Binary (true or false): "The software is fully compliant with industry security standards."

---

### How to write one

**Step 1: Start with the end in mind.**
Picture the task finished. Think in outcomes, not steps.

**Step 2: Write the statement.**
Use the present tense. Describe the result. Make it measurable or binary where you can.

---

### When you need more than one

Sometimes one statement covers it. Sometimes you need a set.

- **Major Done Statement:** the broad goal.
- **Sub-statements:** the specific parts needed to reach it.

This lets you track progress in detail. It also helps you check that your planned actions are enough to cause the outcome.

**Example: documenting a team process**

First, spot the action ("create documentation") and ask: *Why do I want this?* You find two reasons, so you split them.

- **Major Done Statement:** We have documentation that explains how to run our three most used reports.
  - **Sub-statement 1:** I no longer have to explain how to run the same report again and again.
  - **Sub-statement 2:** Everyone on the team can run our three most used reports quickly and accurately.

---

### Tips for better Done Statements

- **Use simple present tense.** Example: "The application is responsive."
- **Leave room for tactics.** Say "load time is reduced by 50%," not "refactor the code using X method." Let the team pick the path.
- **Pair with an action only when needed.** Use this when a result needs one specific method, or when the person lacks the experience to choose. Still phrase it as an outcome.
- **Do not dress up a completed action.** "I have reviewed the document" is just a task. If there is a real reason behind it, state that as the outcome.

**Watch the wording.** All three below are measurable, but only the third links to the real goal:

1. "We have eliminated more than 90% of the causes of rework." *(focuses on the action of eliminating)*
2. "We have monitoring that finds the causes of rework more than 90% of the time." *(focuses on the mechanism)*
3. "More than 90% of jobs are done right the first time." *(focuses on the actual outcome)*

Aim for number three.

## Patterns vs anti-patterns

| Do this (pattern) | Avoid this (anti-pattern) |
|---|---|
| **Outcome over action:** "The software meets all functional and non-functional requirements before release." | **Action-oriented:** "The development team works hard on software quality." |
| **Clear purpose:** "Our new hires speed up roadmap progress by 30%." | **No purpose:** "We have more people working for us." |
| **Flexible:** "The product documentation is ready for users." | **Over-prescriptive:** "Prepare the documentation as a Quick Start Guide." |
| **Simple tense:** "The software is bug-free." | **Vague tense:** "The software has consistently been proving itself free from bugs." |
| **Objective:** "90% of critical bugs are resolved within 48 hours." | **Ambiguous:** "We resolve critical bugs quickly." |
| **Outcome, not done action:** "Code revisions lead to a 10% drop in the need for revisions." | **Action worded as done:** "We have finished building a new code review process." |

---

## What to be careful of

This is where most people slip. Watch for these traps.

- **Relabeling an output as an outcome.** Renaming "10 workshops delivered" to sound like a result does not make it one. The change has to be real.
- **Vague outcomes.** Phrases like "a better experience" or "improved efficiency" sound nice but give no direction. No one can tell when you are done. Add a number or a clear true/false condition.
- **No baseline.** "Reduce load time by 50%" only works if you know the starting point. Measure before you start.
- **Hard to measure the change.** Some outcomes are tough to track, and other factors muddy the result. Pick a metric you can actually observe and tie to your work.
- **Confusing finished with improved.** Closing the ticket is not proof of benefit. Always ask: did anything get better?
- **Holding scope too tight.** Output thinking locks you into a fixed list. When you learn something mid-project, you are afraid to change it. Outcome thinking lets you adjust the "how" as long as the result holds.
- **A culture that punishes learning.** Outcome focus needs room to test, learn, and sometimes fail. Without that, people retreat to ticking off tasks.
- **Forcing two goals into one statement.** If a statement has two separate effects, split it. Each Done Statement should stand on its own.

---

## Self-evaluation checklist

Run your statement through these five questions:

1. Is the end outcome obvious just from reading it?
2. If there is a purpose beyond the action, does the statement show it?
3. Can someone else confirm it is done?
4. Does it allow any tactic to reach the outcome?
5. Is it simple, direct, and free of clutter?

Then do two more checks:

- **Get feedback.** Ask others to read it. If they are confused, refine it.
- **Reflect.** Did it help you reach the goal without endless back-and-forth or rework? If yes, your approach is working.

---

## Where Done Statements fit

Done Statements are part of a wider shift from **outputs to outcomes**.

- An **output** is what you deliver: a feature, a document, a report.
- An **outcome** is the change that follows: faster work, higher adoption, fewer errors.

This is the same idea behind OKRs (where key results measure outcomes, not task lists) and behind a strong "definition of done." The common failure across all of them is the same: teams measure activity instead of impact.

A Done Statement is your simple tool to stay on the outcome side.
