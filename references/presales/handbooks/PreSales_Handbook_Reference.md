## Structure of the Book

Each chapter is a separate module. You can start anywhere. Reading in order gives you the full picture, but you can skip chapters that are not relevant right now.

The structure follows the **Tell-Show-Tell** method: explain the idea, go into detail, then give key takeaways. Every chapter ends with a summary for fast review.

## Chapter 1 — What Is PreSales and Why It Matters in SaaS

PreSales is the process between a potential client's first interest and their decision to purchase.

It covers:
- Client needs assessment
- Solution demonstrations
- Technical consultations
- Tailored solution design
- Proof of concept (PoC) development

In SaaS, clients rent software rather than own it. The PreSales team helps prospects understand how the software solves their problems and whether it fits their needs.

**Core PreSales activities:**
- Figure out what the client needs — ask questions to understand the problems they are trying to solve.
- Qualify the opportunity — ensure your solution fits their requirements.
- Show the ropes — give demos to show how the software helps.
- Give expert advice — explain technical details and integrations.
- Tailor the experience — build or show a version of the solution specific to their context.

### Why PreSales Exists

- Helps clients understand complex products by turning technical details into clear value propositions.
- Reduces post-sale conflicts by aligning product capabilities with client needs before the sale.
- Acts as a bridge between technical teams and client language.
- Increases the likelihood of a successful sale by setting clear expectations.
- Feeds client insights back to product development.

### Key Challenges in PreSales

- Understanding client needs in complex and changing environments.
- Translating complex product features into simple value propositions.
- Balancing customisation vs. configuration decisions.
- Navigating coordination between sales, product, marketing, and client service.
- Managing long B2B sales cycles while maintaining momentum.
- Staying current with changing technology and market trends.

### Key Takeaways

- **Discovery** — Engage with potential clients to uncover needs, challenges, and aspirations.
- **Develop a Champion** — Identify and nurture someone inside the client organisation who advocates for your solution.
- **Enable your Champion** — Give them the tools, data, and narratives to promote your product internally.
- **Deliver a Great Demo** — Tell a story where the product solves tangible business challenges.
- **Trusted Advisor** — Position yourself not just as a seller but as a genuine advisor.
- **Team Sport** — Collaborate with sales, product, client service, and the client.
- **Prove Your Value** — Articulate why choosing your product is better than the status quo.
- **Learn Your Lessons** — Every interaction offers something to learn and improve.

## Chapter 2 — The Role of a PreSales Professional

The PreSales professional sits between product and client. You translate complexity into clarity, and features into value.

**Core activities:**
- Lead discovery conversations
- Run software demonstrations
- Build business cases and ROI models
- Answer technical and functional questions
- Support RFPs and proof of concepts
- Develop and enable internal champions

**You are a trusted advisor, not just a demo machine.**

## Chapter 3 — The Buying / Selling Journey

Understanding the buying journey is essential. Clients move through stages — awareness, consideration, evaluation, decision — and your job is to guide them forward at each step.

**Key principle:** The client must sell the solution internally to their decision board. PreSales must equip champions to do that effectively.

## Chapter 4 — Developing a Champion

A champion is someone inside the client organisation who believes in your solution and advocates for it internally.

### How to Develop a Champion

- **Build trust and relationships** — Invest time in understanding their role, challenges, and objectives. Show genuine interest in their success.
- **Provide tailored resources** — Give them customised materials, demos, and access to information they can use internally.
- **Engage in regular communication** — Stay in touch with updates, check-ins, and industry insights.
- **Empower their advocacy** — Offer training, success stories, and detailed use cases they can use to pitch internally.
- **Acknowledge and appreciate** — A thank-you note or acknowledgement of their support reinforces their commitment.
- **Involve them in the process** — Include them in key discussions and strategy meetings.
- **Align with their goals** — Show how your solution helps them achieve their personal and professional objectives.

### Key Takeaways

- The champion bridges external sales efforts and internal decision-making.
- Identify champions by looking for people with deep interest and influence, even if they are not the most vocal.
- Nurture champions with tailored resources, trust, and regular touchpoints.

## Chapter 5 — Sales Discovery

Sales Discovery is the first step in building a sales relationship. It is an investigation — not a casual chat — designed to understand the prospect's situation, problems, goals, and needs.

In most cases, Sales handles discovery. PreSales supports when requirements are complex or when sales is new.

### The BANT Framework

BANT is a structured method to guide the discovery process.

**Budget** — Understand the financial capability and willingness of the client. Knowing their budget ensures you propose solutions within their financial reality.

**Authority** — Identify who has the power to say yes. Engaging the right decision-maker avoids wasted time and stalled processes.

**Needs** — Uncover the client's specific challenges, requirements, and pain points. This is the core of discovery. Read between the lines — understand what they say and what they don't say.

**Timeline** — Understand when the client plans to implement or decide. Align your sales process with their internal timeline.

### Demos During Discovery

Avoid running demos during Sales Discovery. The focus of this phase is understanding, not showing.

Jumping into a demo too early can:
- Distract from learning about the client's real needs.
- Create misaligned expectations about what the solution can do.
- Lead to a less impactful demonstration later.

If a client insists on seeing something, use demo automation or a high-level visual overview instead of a full product demo.

### Documentation — OSD and CRM

The **Opportunity Scoping Document (OSD)** captures everything from the discovery phase:
- Key challenges and pain points
- Budget information and constraints
- Decision-makers and stakeholders
- Implementation timelines

Store and maintain this in your CRM so the whole team has access. Tools like Gong.io can analyse conversations and provide deeper insights.

## Chapter 6 — Qualify Early, Qualify Hard

Not every opportunity deserves full PreSales effort. Qualify hard before investing time in demos, POCs, and proposals.

Ask yourself:
- Is there a real budget?
- Do we have access to the decision-maker?
- Does our solution genuinely solve their problem?
- Is there a realistic timeline?

Qualify out early if the answer to these is no. It saves time for both sides.

## Chapter 7 — Functional and Technical Discovery

This goes deeper than Sales Discovery. It focuses on the technical environment, workflows, integrations, and specific requirements.

**Key areas to cover:**
- Current software infrastructure and tech stack
- Integration points with existing systems
- Data migration needs
- Security and compliance requirements
- Team sizes, roles, and workflows
- Pain points with current systems
- Future scalability needs

Use a structured questionnaire or the OSD to document everything.

## Chapter 8 — The Opportunity Scoping Document (OSD)

The OSD is the central document that captures all discovery insights. It bridges the client's needs and your proposed solution.

It should include:
- Company and team overview
- Business challenges and pain points
- Technical environment and requirements
- Budget constraints
- Decision-making stakeholders
- Timeline and milestones
- Agreed next steps

Share the OSD with the client to confirm alignment. Update it as the conversation evolves.

## Chapter 10 — The Demo (Tell the Story)

The demo is not a feature tour. It is a story where your product solves the client's specific challenges.

### Tell-Show-Tell Framework

1. **Tell** — Set the context. Explain what you are going to show and why it matters.
2. **Show** — Demonstrate the solution in the context of the client's real-world scenario.
3. **Tell** — Recap what you showed and connect it back to the business impact.

### Before the Demo

- Understand the Top 3-5 things the client cares about.
- Prepare and rehearse thoroughly.
- Test all technology in advance.
- Send a prep email with the agenda and expectations.

### During the Demo

- Start with the most important topic.
- Use real-world scenarios and personas. Say "I'm Kevin, I do..." — not "you can do this."
- Encourage participation and ask questions.
- Take breaks every 45-60 minutes.
- Reserve at least 10 minutes for questions and next steps.

### After the Demo

- Call each participant individually to gather feedback.
- Share the recording and materials.
- Answer any open questions.
- Schedule a follow-up to define next steps.

See the **Cheat Sheet — Demo** for the full checklist.

## Chapter 12 — Return on Investment (ROI) and Value Calculation

A strong business case helps clients justify the investment internally and accelerates the decision.

**To build an ROI model:**
- Quantify the cost of the current problem (time lost, errors, manual effort).
- Calculate the value your solution delivers (time saved, revenue gained, risk reduced).
- Show the expected payback period and net benefit over time.
- Use the client's own data wherever possible — it is more credible.

Present ROI in the language of the economic buyer: financial impact, cost savings, revenue growth.

## Chapter 13 — Proof of Concept (PoC) Execution

A PoC is a structured test to prove that your solution works in the client's specific environment.

**When to run a PoC:**
- The client needs to validate technical fit before committing.
- There are specific integration or performance requirements to prove.

**Keys to a successful PoC:**
- Define success criteria upfront and in writing. Both sides must agree on what "done" looks like.
- Set a clear timeline and scope. Avoid scope creep.
- Assign a dedicated point of contact on both sides.
- Debrief after the PoC — review results against the agreed criteria and define next steps.

## Chapter 14 — RFX Process (RFI / RFP / RFQ)

RFX responses require close collaboration between PreSales, Sales, and Product.

**Key principles:**
- Qualify the RFX before responding. Is it worth the effort? Do you have a real chance of winning?
- Understand the scoring criteria before you write a single word.
- Tailor every response to the client's specific needs. Generic answers lose.
- Be honest about gaps. Overpromising creates problems post-sale.
- Use the discovery insights from the OSD to make responses specific and credible.

## Chapter 15 — Objection Handling

Objections are not rejection. They are requests for more information or reassurance.

**Common objection categories:**
- Price and budget — "It is too expensive."
- Fit — "We are not sure your solution covers our use case."
- Timing — "We are not ready yet."
- Competition — "We are also looking at [competitor]."
- Risk — "What if this doesn't work for us?"

**How to handle objections:**
1. Listen fully. Do not interrupt.
2. Acknowledge the concern genuinely.
3. Clarify to make sure you understand it correctly.
4. Respond with facts, examples, or a direct solution.
5. Confirm that your response resolved the concern.

## Chapter 16 — Closing

Closing is not a trick. It is the natural outcome of a well-executed PreSales process.

**Signs that a deal is ready to close:**
- The client has confirmed their budget and timeline.
- You have access to the decision-maker.
- The champion is active and engaged.
- The PoC or demo has been well received.
- Objections have been addressed.

**Closing techniques:**
- Mutual action plan — Agree on the specific steps to get from now to signed.
- Summarise the value — Recap the business case and what the client gains.
- Address the last concern — Ask directly: "Is there anything else standing in the way?"

## Chapter 17 — Close Won / Close Lost Lessons Learned

After every deal, run a debrief.

**Close Won:**
- What did we do well?
- What was the key decision factor?
- Who was the most important champion?
- What can we replicate next time?

**Close Lost:**
- Why did we lose?
- Was the loss avoidable?
- Did we qualify correctly?
- What did the competitor do better?
- What will we do differently next time?

Document lessons learned and share them with the team.

## Chapter 19 — Documenting and Knowledge Sharing

Knowledge stays trapped in individual heads unless you document it.

**What to document:**
- Discovery notes in the OSD and CRM.
- Demo scripts and recorded demos.
- Objection responses and win/loss analyses.
- Technical integration guides.
- Competitive intelligence.

**Share it openly.** A team that shares knowledge wins together.

## Chapter 20 — Competition

You will always have competitors. Know them well.

**What to understand about competitors:**
- Their strengths and weaknesses.
- How they position their product.
- Where clients typically compare you to them.
- What clients say after switching from them to you (or from you to them).

**Never bad-mouth a competitor.** Instead, focus on where your solution delivers more value for this specific client's needs.

## Chapter 22 — PreSales Leadership

Leading a PreSales team requires a different skillset than doing PreSales.

**Core leadership responsibilities:**
- Hire for communication skills and curiosity, not just technical knowledge.
- Coach your team through deal reviews, demo feedback, and regular 1:1s.
- Build a knowledge-sharing culture. Make it easy for the team to learn from each other.
- Define and track the right KPIs.
- Protect the team from being spread too thin across too many deals.
- Align with Sales leadership on qualification standards and engagement models.

---

## Chapter 23 — Metrics and KPIs for PreSales

**Key metrics to track:**

| Metric | What it measures |
|---|---|
| Win rate | Percentage of deals closed that PreSales supported |
| Technical win rate | Deals where the solution was selected on technical merit |
| Time to demo | How quickly PreSales engages after qualification |
| Demo to PoC conversion | How often a demo leads to a PoC |
| PoC to close rate | How often a PoC leads to a signed deal |
| Average deal size | Revenue from PreSales-supported deals |
| Pipeline coverage | Value of opportunities PreSales is actively supporting |
| Customer satisfaction (CSAT) | Client feedback on PreSales interactions |

Track what matters for your team and business. Review metrics regularly and use them to improve, not just report.
