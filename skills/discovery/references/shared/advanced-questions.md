# Advanced Discovery Questions — Strategic Depth Layer

These questions go beyond the universal opening framework.
Use them when you have established rapport and need to go deeper — particularly for:
- Senior stakeholder or executive-level sessions
- Complex, politically charged, or multi-department deals
- Situations where the deal is stalling or the buying journey feels unclear
- Any time you sense there is a submerged reason why progress is slow

They are organized in the order you would typically encounter them across a discovery conversation.
Do not ask all of them in one session — select 6–8 based on what matters most for this deal.

---

## 01 — Deep Process Diagnostics

Use after the current-state walkthrough. These questions move from surface symptoms to root causes.

| # | Question | Why it matters | Follow-up questions |
|---|----------|----------------|---------------------|
| 1.1 | **What is not working today — and what repeatedly frustrates the team?** | Surfaces undesirable effects without assigning blame. The fastest path from symptoms to a list of real requirements. | Which issues happen most often? Which are most damaging when they do? |
| 1.2 | **Where do you experience the most delays, bottlenecks, or constraints?** | Finds the limiting factor that caps throughput. Improving the wrong area leaves results flat — this focuses your value. | Is the constraint a resource, a policy, a knowledge gap, or a measurement problem? What sets the pace for everything else? |
| 1.3 | **How are you working around this problem today?** | Workarounds reveal true requirements. They show what users will not tolerate, expose hidden costs, and surface the real incumbent — usually spreadsheets, not a competitor. | Which spreadsheets, email chains, or side tools exist to compensate? Who maintains them? What breaks when that person is out? |
| 1.4 | **What is the single most frustrating part of this process for you personally?** | Personal frustration drives action. Company-level pain can stay abstract. When you link your solution to something the individual dislikes every day, you create a personal champion. | When does it usually happen? What do you do in the moment when it occurs? |
| 1.5 | **Where does the data come from today, and where does it need to go?** | Validates end-to-end information flow. Poor data ownership, timing, or destination is a leading root cause of the inefficiencies you will be asked to solve. | Who owns each data source? What happens when data arrives late or incorrect? Which downstream decisions break first? |
| 1.6 | **Why do you think this problem happens — and what stops it from being fixed today?** | Moves from symptoms to causes and to the blockers that sustain them. You often find policies, incentive misalignment, or structural constraints that a software tool alone cannot solve. | What assumptions are we all operating under? Is there a policy or organisational structure that perpetuates this? |
| 1.7 | **Do multiple problems you have described share the same root cause?** | One fix that removes several pains is a more compelling business case. This question also simplifies scope and focuses where to start. | If you changed one thing, what would remove the most issues at once? |
| 1.8 | **What two goals are you trying to achieve that seem to conflict with each other?** | Uncovers hidden tradeoffs that block progress — speed vs. compliance, cost vs. service level, central control vs. local autonomy. Surfacing the conflict allows you to design a solution that resolves it rather than forces a choice. | What do you need from goal A? What do you need from goal B? What is the assumption that says you cannot have both? |

## 02 — Data Readiness & Trust

Use when the conversation moves toward implementation scope or when data quality concerns have surfaced.

| # | Question | Why it matters | Follow-up questions |
|---|----------|----------------|---------------------|
| 2.1 | **Where do people not trust the data today?** | Data mistrust leads to poor adoption and slow decisions even if the software is excellent. Separating tool problems from data problems prevents the new system from being blamed for upstream issues. | Which reports or decisions are questioned because of data quality? Who owns fixing those issues? |
| 2.2 | **How confident are you in the quality and ownership of the data used in this process?** | Poor data quality is a leading cause of failed software projects. This question surfaces the gap between assumed and actual readiness before it becomes a scope and timeline risk. | Where do people rely on their own offline copies rather than the system of record? Who is accountable for data quality today? |
| 2.3 | **What data issues would cause adoption to fail even if the system works perfectly?** | Exposes silent failure risks. Prevents the software from being blamed for problems that exist upstream. | Which data sources are most fragile or disputed? What manual corrections exist today? |

## 03 — Impact & Strategic Urgency

Use to connect operational pain to executive metrics. These questions raise the conversation from process to business case.

| # | Question | Why it matters | Follow-up questions |
|---|----------|----------------|---------------------|
| 3.1 | **How is this impacting your ability to hit your quarterly or annual goals?** | Connects a process problem to the metrics leadership tracks. This shift from "operational issue" to "strategic blocker" is what gets budget approved. | Which goals are most at risk? How large is the gap? How visible is it to senior leadership? |
| 3.2 | **What have you tried before to solve this — and why did it not work?** | Prevents repeating a failed approach. Exposes constraints (budget, ownership, culture, data quality) that a vendor must address differently this time. | What did you learn from those attempts? What must be different this time for it to succeed? |
| 3.3 | **If you implemented the right solution, what would you do with the time or resources you would free up?** | Moves the prospect to imagine the future and self-articulate the upside. Reveals what higher-value work is currently blocked — and makes the ROI personal, not abstract. | What higher-priority work is not getting done today because of this? What would you focus on first? |
| 3.4 | **What early wins would demonstrate this was the right decision in 30, 60, and 90 days?** | Creates short-term proof of value that protects the project politically after go-live. Weak early results are the most common cause of post-go-live abandonment. | What must happen in the first month to build confidence? Which quick win matters most to leadership? |

## 04 — Decision Risk & Buying Dynamics

These are the questions most SCs never ask. They are the difference between a deal that closes and a deal that dies at no decision.

| # | Question | Why it matters | Follow-up questions |
|---|----------|----------------|---------------------|
| 4.1 | **What typically causes initiatives like this to stall or die internally — even when everyone agrees they make sense?** | Most deals fail at no decision, not because a competitor won. This question surfaces internal friction (shifting priorities, leadership misalignment, fear of change, absent ownership) early enough to address it. | What caused this to happen on a previous initiative? Who would need to be actively sponsoring this to keep it alive? |
| 4.2 | **What would cause this project to pause or stop even if everyone agrees it is the right thing to do?** | Identifies hidden kill criteria — budget freezes, leadership changes, risk aversion, or a competing initiative that absorbs all attention. Helps you understand how fragile the deal really is. | What signals would tell you internally that it is at risk? How would you know early? |
| 4.3 | **What options are you considering besides this — including staying as you are?** | Reveals the real competition, which is often the status quo or internal build, not another vendor. Without understanding what you are competing against, you cannot position value correctly. | What do you find attractive about each option? What concerns you? |
| 4.4 | **If you chose to do nothing, why would that feel like the safer option?** | Forces the prospect to articulate the comfort of inaction. Helps you address the emotional and political reasons for stalling — not just the logical ones. | What risk feels lower if you do nothing? What risk feels higher? |

## 05 — Internal Alignment & Champion Enablement

Use when you have identified a champion. These questions help you understand what they need to sell internally when you are not in the room.

| # | Question | Why it matters | Follow-up questions |
|---|----------|----------------|---------------------|
| 5.1 | **How will you explain and justify this initiative internally when you present it to leadership or finance?** | Your champion must sell this without you. This question uncovers the narrative, language, and evidence they need — so you can build it for them. | What questions do you expect from leadership or finance? What numbers or proof will they want? |
| 5.2 | **What would leadership need to believe for this to get approved quickly?** | Identifies belief gaps, not feature gaps. Helps you tailor your messaging to the executive concerns that matter — risk, ROI, strategic alignment, or precedent. | Who specifically needs to believe this? What is their biggest concern right now? |
| 5.3 | **Where do you expect disagreement or resistance around the value of this initiative?** | Surfaces political tension early so you can address objections proactively rather than after a surprise blocker kills momentum. | Who disagrees and why? What would change their perspective? |

## 06 — Implementation, Adoption & Change Capacity

Use when the conversation moves toward scoping and timelines. These questions protect the project from post-go-live failure.

| # | Question | Why it matters | Follow-up questions |
|---|----------|----------------|---------------------|
| 6.1 | **What constraints do we need to plan around — timeline, IT capacity, data readiness, or change windows?** | Makes the implementation plan realistic. Projects fail most often due to hidden constraints: blackout periods, limited IT availability, or data that is not ready. | Are there freeze periods or release windows? Who will be the internal project lead? How much IT bandwidth is available? |
| 6.2 | **What worries you most about rollout and adoption?** | Adoption is harder than implementation. Asking this question early signals you take change management seriously — and surfaces risks before they become scope surprises. | Who needs the most support through the change? What adoption or training model has worked well in the past? |
| 6.3 | **What other major initiatives or changes are competing for the same resources right now?** | Even strong projects fail when organisations are overloaded. This question assesses whether the company can absorb another major change at this time. | Which initiative takes priority if resources conflict? What would cause this one to be paused? |
| 6.4 | **How much change fatigue exists in the team right now?** | Adoption risk increases when teams are exhausted by recent change. Knowing this lets you adjust rollout scope, phasing, and expectations before the project starts. | What recent changes were hardest on the team and why? What would make this feel different? |
| 6.5 | **Who is accountable for outcomes after go-live — not just system ownership, but the actual business results?** | Clear accountability is required to realize value. Without ownership of results, projects succeed technically but fail to deliver business impact. | Who owns the KPIs after go-live? How are they measured and reported? |

## 07 — Organisational Dynamics & Strategic Frame

Use in executive sessions or when you need to understand the systemic context, not just the immediate project.

| # | Question | Why it matters | Follow-up questions |
|---|----------|----------------|---------------------|
| 7.1 | **Where are teams optimising their local work in ways that might be hurting the end-to-end flow?** | Local optimisation often reduces overall performance. This question identifies misaligned metrics and siloed incentives that block system-level improvement — and elevates the value of an integrated solution. | Which metric drives behaviour that looks right locally but is wrong for the business overall? |
| 7.2 | **Why do initiatives fail in your organisation even when the technology is good?** | Identifies systemic risk factors: weak sponsorship, poor data, unrealistic timelines, or lack of adoption. Lets you design the project from the outset to avoid known failure patterns. | What warning signs usually appear early? What gets underestimated most often? |
| 7.3 | **If a competitor solved this better than you today, what would they be doing differently?** | Reframes the problem strategically and creates external urgency. Connects the initiative to competitive advantage rather than internal operations. | Where would they be faster, cheaper, or more responsive? What would that cost you in the market? |
| 7.4 | **What would leadership need to see to believe this investment was worth it?** | Clarifies the proof needed to convert leadership from conditional to committed. Helps you design the right pilot scope, ROI model, and reference metrics. | What form of evidence carries most weight — internal metrics, peer references, or a live benchmark? |
| 7.5 | **If this initiative is wildly successful, what will people say was the real reason it worked?** | Reveals the true success factors that go beyond features — executive sponsorship, simplicity, timing, data quality, change management. Helps you protect what actually matters. | What would cause the opposite outcome? What must be in place and protected from day one? |
| 7.6 | **If this initiative succeeds, how does it make you personally successful?** | Personal upside strengthens sponsorship and ownership. Ties the initiative to the stakeholder's reputation, career trajectory, or visibility with leadership. | What outcome would make you most proud of this decision? What would disappoint you even if the project delivered on paper? |
