# Discovery Techniques & Facilitation Guide

Practical techniques for running effective discovery sessions.
Read before generating questions — these govern how to ask, not what to ask.

---

## Why Technique Matters

Knowing the right questions is necessary but not sufficient.
The way you ask — your pacing, your listening, your reactions — determines whether the prospect opens up or stays guarded.

A discovery session with good technique and mediocre questions will outperform a session with great questions and poor technique.
The best SCs treat discovery as a conversation, not an interrogation.

---

## Active Listening

Active listening is the single most important discovery skill.
It is not waiting for your turn to speak — it is demonstrating that you are processing what is being said.

**Signals that show you are listening:**
- Pause 2–3 seconds after the prospect finishes before responding. The first answer is rarely the full answer — silence invites more.
- Reflect back in their words, not yours: *"So if I heard you right, the real issue is that the data exists but nobody trusts it — is that accurate?"*
- Acknowledge emotion, not just content: *"That sounds genuinely frustrating — how long has the team been dealing with that?"*
- Write things down visibly (or say you are): *"Let me capture that — that's an important point."*

**What kills active listening:**
- Starting your next question before they have finished answering
- Moving to the next topic the moment you have the data point you wanted
- Nodding and saying "great" after every answer — it signals you are on autopilot
- Typing audibly on a laptop while they are speaking

## The 5 Whys

The 5 Whys is a root cause technique. Use it when you hear a symptom and want to understand what is underneath it.
Each "why" takes you one layer deeper. You rarely need all five.

**How to use it naturally:**
Do not ask "why" five times in a row — it feels like an interrogation. Use varied phrasing:

| Layer | Phrase |
|-------|--------|
| 1st | "Why does that happen?" |
| 2nd | "What causes that?" |
| 3rd | "And what's behind that?" |
| 4th | "So if that changed, would the original problem go away — or is there something deeper?" |
| 5th | "What is the real reason this hasn't been fixed before now?" |

**Example:**
> "Our shipments are always late." →
> Why? "Carriers don't have the booking in time." →
> Why? "We don't finalise the order until the morning of shipment." →
> Why? "The ERP doesn't confirm inventory until it is physically counted." →
> Why? "We have no real-time inventory sync — it's a manual overnight batch." →
> Why hasn't that been fixed? "We tried once but IT said it was too complex to build with our legacy WMS."

The root cause is not carrier performance — it is a data latency and integration gap. That is where the solution lives.

## Empathy Mapping

Empathy mapping means stepping into the stakeholder's position and asking: what are they thinking, feeling, saying, and doing?

Use this **before** the call to anticipate what a stakeholder cares about, given their role and the context you know:

| Dimension | Questions to prepare |
|-----------|---------------------|
| **Thinks** | What are their biggest pressures this quarter? What keeps them up at night? |
| **Feels** | What are they afraid of? What are they proud of or protective of? |
| **Says** | What language do they use publicly? What do they emphasise to leadership? |
| **Does** | What does their day actually look like? What decisions do they make repeatedly? |

**In-session application:**
- When someone gives a guarded answer, map back to their role: *"From a CFO's perspective, I imagine visibility into landed costs matters — is that right?"*
- When you sense discomfort, name it gently: *"I get the impression there's something more to this — is there context I'm missing?"*

## The 5 Whys Applied to Buying Decisions

The 5 Whys also applies to buying decisions, not just process problems.
When a deal stalls, use it internally:

> "Why is the deal not moving?" → "They haven't approved budget." →
> "Why?" → "The CFO hasn't endorsed it." →
> "Why?" → "Our champion hasn't presented it yet." →
> "Why?" → "They don't have the business case language they need." →
> "Why?" → "We haven't given it to them."

Most stalled deals are a discovery failure, not a product failure.

## Demo in Discovery

When a prospect asks for a demo before discovery is complete, they are expressing excitement — not a directive.
Honour the enthusiasm without abandoning the process.

**The risk of an early demo:**
Demonstrating before you understand the environment means showing generic features to a prospect who has specific problems.
You may demonstrate the wrong module, at the wrong depth, to the wrong audience.
It is the equivalent of prescribing medication before diagnosing the patient.

**How to handle the request:**

| Situation | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Prospect is a new contact with no prior context | Offer a **Look & Feel Demo** — a 15-minute overview that conveys scale and capability without committing to a specific workflow. Frame it as: *"Let me give you a high-level tour so you can see the kind of problem space we operate in — and then let's dig into your situation specifically."* |
| Prospect has seen a demo from a competitor | Ask what they saw and what stood out. Then selectively show one or two capability areas that address their known gaps — but tie each to a discovery question: *"Does your current process have this problem?"* |
| Executive is asking for a demo in a first call | Show 2–3 outcomes, not features. Executives buy outcomes. Keep it to 10 minutes and then redirect: *"I want to make sure the rest of the time is focused on your priorities — can I ask a few questions?"* |

**Integrating discovery into the demo:**
When you do demo early, turn it into a discovery tool:
- Ask *"Does this match what you have today, or is yours different?"* after each step
- Ask *"Which of these would be most relevant to your team?"*
- Pause and say *"Before I go further — I want to make sure I'm showing the right things. What problem are you most trying to solve?"*

The best discovery often happens during a demo, because seeing real software surfaces assumptions the prospect did not know they had.

## Translating Discovery into Demo Focus

After discovery is complete, the findings should directly govern what you show and how you frame it.

| Discovery finding | Demo implication |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Pain: manual re-keying between ERP and TMS | Demo the integration layer and automated data flow — not the UI |
| KPI: reduce customs clearance time by 30% | Open with the outcome dashboard and work backwards to the process |
| Personal pain: SC Manager drowning in exception emails | Demo exception management and alerting — make it feel like relief |
| Decision criterion: audit trail for trade compliance | Show the compliance log and document history up front |
| Competitive pressure: incumbent is Excel + email | Demo the contrast — show the before/after, not just the after |
| Status quo bias: "we tried this before and it failed" | Demo the specific thing that failed before and show it works now |

Every demo section should begin with: *"You told us that [pain]. Here is how [product] addresses that."*
Never start a demo section without anchoring it to something the prospect said.

## Handling a Guarded or Reluctant Stakeholder

Some prospects give short answers, deflect questions, or seem disengaged.

**Common causes:**
- They have been burned by vendor-led discovery that felt like a sales pitch
- They are not the decision maker and are unsure why they are in the room
- They are under time pressure and want to get to the point
- They have already decided on another vendor and are going through motions

**Techniques:**
- **Slow down and be transparent:** *"I want to make sure this time is genuinely useful for you — what would make it worth your while?"*
- **Give before you ask:** Share one specific insight you found during research before asking your first question. This demonstrates you have done the work and are not just fishing.
- **Ask about them, not the project:** The opening question about their role is not optional — it is the trust-building mechanism. Do not skip it.
- **Acknowledge the dynamic:** If someone seems guarded, name it directly but softly: *"I get the sense there is something we should address before we dive in — is there anything on your mind?"*
- **Offer to stop early:** *"I have about 10 more minutes of questions — but I am also happy to use this time differently if something else is more useful for you."* Paradoxically, offering to stop often makes people more willing to continue.

## Using Silence

Silence is a discovery tool, not a gap to fill.

After asking a question, wait. Count to 3 silently before saying anything.
When a prospect finishes an answer and pauses, wait again — they are often about to add the most important thing.

**What silence communicates:**
- You are comfortable with complexity
- You are not rushing to a solution
- You trust them to have more to say

The reflex to fill silence is a signal you are more interested in talking than listening.
