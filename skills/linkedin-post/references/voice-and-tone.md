# Voice and tone

The voice craft that keeps content sounding like a person, not a platform. **Applies to all
profiles**, and the humanization pass consults it. The **`personal`** profile leans on it
hardest (it has no brand voice to fall back on), but the principles harden branded (`company`) output too.

## Sound like yourself, not LinkedIn

**The test:** read the draft aloud. Does it sound like you explaining something to a smart
colleague over coffee, or like "a LinkedIn post"? If the latter, rewrite.

Too LinkedIn-y:
> "I'm thrilled to share that after an incredible journey of growth, I've discovered a
> game-changing framework that will transform how you think about this."

Actually human:
> "We spent three months trying to make it work. Most of that time went on the wrong problems.
> Here's what we missed."

## First person, direct, no hedging

Write in first person. Be direct. Don't stack qualifiers for politeness.

Hedged:
> "It might be worth considering that perhaps the traditional approach could potentially be
> somewhat less effective than some alternatives."

Direct:
> "The traditional approach wastes time. Here's what works better."

Acknowledge real uncertainty ("I'm not sure this holds outside B2B"), but don't hedge to soften.

## Show expertise through specifics, not claims

Claiming:
> "As a seasoned professional with years of experience, I can tell you that..."

Demonstrating:
> "After 14 discovery calls on trade compliance this year, the pattern I see most is..."

The specifics (14 calls, this year) do more than any credential.

## Tone calibration

- **Confident, not arrogant.** "This is how I approach it. Other valid approaches exist, but
  this one has worked consistently," not "Most people get this wrong, let me explain why I'm right."
- **Scope your claims.** "This worked for our logistics use case, I haven't tested it
  elsewhere," not "This is the only way that works."
- **Genuine enthusiasm, not performed excitement.** "This is the feature I've wanted for two
  years, it changes how I work every week," not "SO EXCITED to announce this INCREDIBLE thing!!"

## Kill the buzzwords and corporate speak

Strategic, results-oriented, proven track record, synergy, leverage, value-add, best-of-breed,
cutting-edge, revolutionary, disruptive, rockstar, ninja, guru, thought leader, ecosystem,
landscape, space, move the needle, circle back, take offline. Prefer "products" or "tools"
over "solutions".

## Patterns that read as fake

- **Humble-bragging.** "So humbled to be featured in..." / "Never expected this little PoC to
  become a $2M deal." Either state the accomplishment plainly or leave it out.
- **Condescension.** "For those who don't understand..." / "Let me explain this simply..." /
  "Most people don't realise..." Assume your audience is smart.
- **Performative vulnerability.** Open with struggle, describe the journey, reveal success,
  tidy lesson. This arc is now a recognised template. Share a failure only when the failure
  itself is instructive, not as a setup for a win.

## Assume intelligence, not knowledge

Your reader is smart but may not know your domain. Explain the "what" and "why," not just the "how".

Too technical:
> "A multi-modal classification pipeline with 94% precision on 6-digit HS code assignment."

Too dumbed down:
> "We made the AI better at finding the right code!"

Right balance:
> "We automated the part classifiers get stuck on, assigning the 6-digit code for manufactured
> goods. Result: 94% precision on our test set, which beats most manual processes."

## Before you post

1. **Would I say this out loud?** Read it aloud. Does it sound like you?
2. **Where are the specifics?** Point to dates, numbers, names, quotes.
3. **What's the intent?** Story, observation, take, teach, react, or a genuine question?
4. **Any template residue?** Phrases lifted from a "how to LinkedIn" guide?
5. **Does it end naturally?** Or did you bolt on an engagement CTA?

If something feels off, it probably is. Rewrite until it sounds like you.
