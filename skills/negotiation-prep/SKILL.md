# Negotiation Prep

Builds your brief before any commercial discussion.
Know your positions, your levers, and your walk-away point before you enter the room.
Never improvise these in a live negotiation.

## Step 1 — Define your three positions for every variable

Before any negotiation, you need three numbers for each thing that might be discussed.
Write these down before the meeting — not during it.

```
YOUR POSITIONS

Variable              | Ideal (what you'd love) | Acceptable (you can sign here) | Walk-away (below this = no deal)
----------------------|------------------------|-------------------------------|----------------------------------
Price / contract value|                        |                               |
Payment terms         |                        |                               |
Implementation scope  |                        |                               |
Contract length       |                        |                               |
Go-live timeline      |                        |                               |
Other: [variable]     |                        |                               |

The walk-away point is the most important number on this table.
Know it before you go in — not while you're sitting across from the customer.
```

## Step 2 — Understand their position

What do you know (or can reasonably infer) about what the customer wants?

```
THEIR LIKELY POSITIONS

What they've said they want:
[From discovery, from the demo close, from prior conversations]

What they probably NEED (may differ from what they say):
[Your honest assessment — budget cycle end, go-live date, internal approval threshold]

Their real constraints:
[Budget cycle timing, competing priorities, internal procurement rules, EB approval level]

Who has the authority to actually close this:
[Name the Economic Buyer — if they're not in the room, the negotiation has limited value]

Are they under internal pressure to reduce cost?
[If yes — is it a real constraint or a negotiation tactic?]
```

## Step 3 — Your trade levers

Negotiation is not about giving things away. It is about trading things you value less
for things you value more. Never give a concession without receiving something in return.

```
WHAT WE CAN OFFER                       | WHAT WE WANT IN RETURN
----------------------------------------|------------------------------------------
Extended payment terms (e.g. quarterly) | Multi-year commitment / Faster close
Phased pricing (pilot first)            | Full contract pre-signed for Phase 2
Additional training days (1–2 days)     | Named reference customer agreement
Priority support tier upgrade           | Accelerated paper/legal process
Pilot at reduced scope                  | Commitment to full evaluation criteria sign-off
[Other concession]                      | [What we need for it]

RULE: Every concession has a trade. Giving for free signals the original price wasn't real.
```

## Step 4 — Concession sequence

Plan the ORDER in which you will concede. The first concession sets the pattern.
Don't improvise this in the room.

```
CONCESSION SEQUENCE

First concession (if needed):
[Choose the lowest cost to us, highest perceived value to them]
"I've had a look at what I can do. If we move forward this [month/quarter], I can [X]."
→ Make it feel significant even if the dollar value is small
→ Pause after offering — don't keep talking

Second concession (only if the first didn't close it):
[One step up in cost — signal you are genuinely reaching your limit]
→ Slow down between concessions. Speed signals there is more room.
→ "I've gone further than I expected to. This is genuinely where I land."

Final position:
[What you will hold at — stated calmly and with conviction, not apologetically]
"I've gone as far as I can go on [X]. What I can still offer is [Y]."

If they push further:
"Help me understand what's driving the ask — is it the total number, the payment structure,
or something else? I want to find a way through, but I need to understand the real constraint."
```

## Step 5 — Responses to the most common negotiation tactics

```
TACTIC: "Your competitor is [X]% cheaper"
Response:
"I'd expect them to lead on price — that's often how this plays out.
Can I ask what you saw in their demo that you felt addressed [specific pain they mentioned]?
Because that's usually where the comparison changes."
[Never defend your price against a number you can't verify. Shift to value.]

TACTIC: "We love it but the price is just too high"
Response:
"Too high relative to what? Help me understand — is it the budget ceiling,
the ROI not being clear enough, or the payment structure?"
[They've told you there IS a yes in there. Diagnose the blocker before responding.]

TACTIC: "We need a decision by end of quarter" (artificial urgency)
Response:
"I want to make sure we can hit that. What has to happen on your side —
is legal aligned, and does [EB name] have this on their calendar for this week?"
[Test whether the deadline is real before discounting to hit it.]

TACTIC: "Can you do [X]% off?"
Response:
"Let me see what I can do. If I can find movement on the investment,
what would you be able to give me in return — a reference, a longer term, a faster close?"
[Never respond to a number with a number immediately. Make them trade first.]

TACTIC: "We need to think about it"
Response:
"Of course — I want you to make the right decision. Can I ask what the one thing is
that still needs to be resolved? Is it commercial, technical, or internal alignment?"
[Always diagnose 'think about it' — it always means something specific.]
```

## Step 6 — Your one-page negotiation brief

Fill this in and take it into the meeting:

```
NEGOTIATION BRIEF — [Account] | [Meeting date]

Our opening position: [One line — what we're going in with]
Their likely opening ask: [What we expect them to request]
Our planned first response: [Written out — not improvised]
Walk-away point: [The number/condition — written before we enter]
Ideal outcome: [What a great meeting looks like — specific]
Who will be in the room from their side: [Names — confirm EB is attending]
What's the one thing that could end this meeting well: [The specific commitment we're seeking]
```
