# Pricing Positioning

How to introduce pricing in a way that lands — always value first, then investment.
Never lead with the number. Never let the number sit alone in silence.

## The one rule

> Never introduce price until the customer has told you what the problem is worth to them.

```
THE RIGHT SEQUENCE — always follow this order:

1. Confirm the pain — in their words, not yours
2. Confirm the value — what fixing it is worth to their business
3. Confirm the urgency — why they need to solve it now
4. Then, and only then, introduce the investment

If you introduce price BEFORE value: you've made it a cost decision.
If you introduce price AFTER value: you've made it a return decision.
These feel like the same conversation. They produce completely different outcomes.
```

---

## Step 1 — Build the value sandwich

Wrap every price mention in value — before AND after the number.

```
BEFORE the number (30–60 seconds):
"Based on what you've told us — [pain 1 in their words] and [pain 2 in their words] —
we'd expect to deliver [value estimate, confidence-tagged].
For a company of your size and trade complexity, that's typically a [timeframe] payback."

THE NUMBER:
"The annual investment for [product/scope] is [amount]."

IMMEDIATELY AFTER the number (do not pause here — continue talking):
"So at [annual cost], against [value estimate] in year one, the business case is roughly
[payback period]. And that's before we count [secondary value driver — risk, team capacity, etc.]."

DO NOT let there be silence after the number.
The pause after a price is when buyers anchor to the cost. Fill it with value immediately.
```

## Step 2 — The ROI anchor (customer-specific)

Every pricing conversation must be anchored to this customer's own numbers — not industry benchmarks.

```
ROI ANCHOR — [Account name]

We've identified [N] value drivers with [Account] during our evaluation:

Value driver                          | Estimate      | Confidence | Payback
--------------------------------------|---------------|------------|--------
[Driver 1 — e.g. Classification time] | $[X] / year   | 🟢/🟡      | [Months]
[Driver 2 — e.g. duty / tariff savings] | $[X] / year | 🟢/🟡      | [Months]
[Driver 3 — risk reduction]           | Qualitative   | 🟡         | —

Total estimated annual value: $[X]
Annual investment:            $[Y]
Simple payback period:        [X] months

Suggested framing:
"On those numbers — acknowledging the actual return will vary by your specific
implementation — what would prevent this from being a straightforward business decision?"
```

## Step 3 — Handling "too expensive"

"Too expensive" almost never means the number is actually wrong.
It usually means one of three things — and each needs a different response.

```
DIAGNOSE BEFORE YOU RESPOND:

"When you say that — is it the total number relative to budget,
is it that the ROI isn't convincing enough yet,
or is it more about the timing this year?"

→ If BUDGET:
  Pivot to: phased pricing, quarterly payments, or a pilot approach that fits their ceiling.
  "Let's look at how we structure the first phase to fit the budget you have available."

→ If ROI not convincing:
  Go back to the value conversation — don't drop the price.
  "Let me make sure I've built this case correctly. Walk me back to [specific pain] —
  what did you say that cost you annually?"

→ If TIMING:
  Understand the real constraint: budget cycle, a competing project, internal approvals.
  "If the timing is the issue, what would need to be true for this to be in the next budget cycle?
  Is there a pilot approach that works within what you have available now?"

IMPORTANT: Never drop the price to respond to "too expensive" without diagnosing first.
Price drops are one-way — you cannot take them back once offered.
```

## Step 4 — Defending against discount pressure

```
WHEN ASKED FOR A DISCOUNT:

Step 1 — Don't panic. Don't say yes immediately.
"Let me see what I can do." [Then pause. Don't fill the silence.]

Step 2 — Ask what they're trading.
"If I can find movement on the investment side, what would help me get there?
A reference customer agreement? A longer commitment? A faster close this quarter?"

Step 3 — If you concede, name the trade explicitly.
"If we can move forward this [month/quarter], I can offer [X].
But I'd need [specific commitment] from your side to make that work."

Step 4 — Anchor back to ROI when they push further.
"I want to make sure this works for both of us.
At the current investment, the payback is [X months].
Before I go further, help me understand what's driving the ask."
```

## Step 5 — Competitive pricing ("they're cheaper")

```
When a customer says a competitor is cheaper:

"I'd expect us to be priced higher — and here's why that matters for your decision.
[One specific, defensible reason our higher price delivers a better return —
NOT a feature list. One thing.]

The real question is: over [contract period], which solution gets [Account]
to [their stated outcome] faster and with less internal effort to get there?
That's where the comparison usually changes."

What NOT to say:
× "We're actually a better value" — this is meaningless without specifics
× "Their price doesn't include everything" — sounds defensive without proof
× "We're in a different category" — sounds like a dodge
```

## Quality checklist

- [ ] Price was introduced AFTER value was confirmed — not before
- [ ] Value sandwich was used — value before number, value immediately after number
- [ ] ROI anchor uses THIS customer's confirmed numbers — not generic industry benchmarks
- [ ] "Too expensive" was diagnosed before any discount was offered
- [ ] Any concession was tied to a trade — not given freely
- [ ] Competitive price comparison uses ONE specific differentiator — not a feature list
- [ ] All value claims are confidence-tagged (🟢 Confirmed / 🟡 Inferred / 🔴 Unknown)
