# Apparel & Footwear — Sector Reference for Supply Chain Mapping

**Cluster:** Consumer
**Representative Companies:** Nike, Inditex (Zara), ASOS, Hugo Boss, Adidas, H&M, PVH, Burberry
**Taxonomy description:** Products that go on the outside of human bodies (contact packaging)

---

## Typical Make/Buy Split
- Fast fashion (Zara/H&M model): 80–100% outsourced (CMT — Cut, Make, Trim; or FOB)
- Sports/performance (Nike, Adidas): 85–95% outsourced to preferred supplier networks; own only design + IP
- Premium / accessible luxury (Hugo Boss): 50–70% outsourced; own ateliers for hero/brand-defining products
- True luxury: 60–80% own controlled workshops / licensed ateliers (craftsmanship is the value)
- Footwear: 85–95% outsourced; own only design and last management
- Key signal: "nominated factories", "sourcing offices", "supplier code of conduct", "tier 1 factories" in sustainability reports

## Inbound Raw Materials — Typical Modal Split
| Material | Mode | Notes |
|----------|------|-------|
| Fabric / woven textiles | Ocean FCL | Bangladesh, Vietnam, China, Turkey, India |
| Knitted fabric | Ocean FCL | China, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka |
| Yarn / fibre (synthetic) | Ocean FCL | Petrochemical-derived; China-dominant |
| Yarn / fibre (natural cotton, wool) | Ocean FCL | US cotton, Australian wool, Indian cotton |
| Trims / accessories (zips, buttons, labels) | Ocean LCL or FCL | Often same-country as factory |
| Leather (standard) | Ocean FCL or Road (Italy) | Italy for premium; Brazil/India for volume |
| Luxury leather | Air or Road (Italy) | High value, time-sensitive |
| Performance / technical fabric | Air or Ocean | Depends on exclusivity and newness |
| Foam / insole components | Road FTL (regional) | Often near factory country |

## Distribution Norms
- Seasonal buying model: 2–4 collections/year with long lead times → inventory spikes
- Fast fashion (Zara model): weekly replenishment from centralised DC (Zaragoza); very short design-to-shelf cycle (2–4 weeks for reactive)
- Sports/performance: mixed model — forward-buys for core; reactive for fast-fashion-style trend items
- Luxury: sell-through model, controlled mono-brand retail, limited discounting, no markdowns (brand damage)
- Returns: 20–35% in ecom (sizing + fit issues) — major reverse logistics cost
- Nearshoring growing: brands nearshoring to Turkey, Morocco, Portugal for faster replenishment of trend items

## Outbound Modal Split
| Channel | Mode | Notes |
|---------|------|-------|
| Own stores | Road FTL (urban) or Air (remote markets) | Controlled replenishment, visual presentation |
| Department stores | Road LTL | Mixed-SKU, hanger or folded |
| Wholesale / multi-brand | Road LTL | Pre-season bulk allocation |
| Ecom / DTC | Parcel express + returns management | B2C single units; highest return rates |
| Outlet / off-price | Road FTL | End-of-season clearance |
| Travel retail | Air or Ocean | Duty-free channels |
| Marketplace (Amazon, Zalando) | Parcel or FBA | Marketplace fulfilment or own stock |

## Air Freight Use Cases
- Trend-reactive replenishment: fast fashion emergency top-up when reorder item goes viral
- Luxury product launches and exclusives: controlled scarcity delivery to VIP markets
- End-of-season recovery: premium products air-freighted to avoid markdown (cost of air < cost of markdown)
- Initial allocation for new season: when ocean lead time misses store opening or key selling window

## Typical Strategic Ambitions
| Dimension | Typical targets |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Growth** | Grow DTC/ecom channel share (higher margin); expand in APAC (China, India) and EM; enter new categories (sports → wellness) |
| **Efficiency** | Reduce order-to-shelf lead time; cut air freight to <5% of shipments; improve return processing cost; drive nearshoring for speed |
| **New Business Models** | Rental / resale / recommerce (circular fashion); subscription styling; personalisation and made-to-measure at scale |
| **Market Share** | Win in athletic leisure (athleisure); defend premium positioning against fast fashion; grow in sustainable segment |
| **Innovation** | Sustainable materials (recycled polyester, plant-based leather, bio-dyes); 3D design to cut sample cycles; digital sampling |
| **Digitalisation** | Real-time sell-through visibility; automated replenishment for core; AI trend sensing for reactive buys |

## Top 10 Use Cases (Business-Driven)
1. **Demand Spike Absorption** — Absorb a 40% demand spike on a viral/trend item within 3 weeks through reactive nearshore sourcing, without stockout
2. **Gross Margin Protection** — Keep gross margin within ±2% despite ocean freight and raw material cost volatility through hedging and nearshore agility
3. **Working Capital on Seasonal Inventory** — Reduce end-of-season markdown rate by 15% through better demand forecasting and in-season replenishment control
4. **Launch on Time** — Deliver new collection to all stores and ecom simultaneously within <1-week variance for 100% of SKUs
5. **Revenue at Risk from Tariff Changes** — Model and mitigate revenue at risk from new trade tariffs on top-3 sourcing countries within 30 days of policy announcement
6. **Ecom Returns Cost** — Reduce ecom returns cost per item by 20% through better size guidance, virtual try-on, and streamlined returns processing
7. **Air Freight Elimination** — Reduce air freight to <4% of total inbound volume through better pre-season buying discipline and nearshore buffer capacity
8. **Supplier Ethical Compliance** — Achieve 100% Tier 1 and 90% Tier 2 supplier audit coverage annually with zero critical violations
9. **Model & React to Sourcing Country Risk** — Re-route 30% of volume from a sanctioned/disrupted sourcing country to alternatives within 6 months
10. **Recommerce Revenue** — Launch and scale resale/recommerce programme to 5% of total brand revenue within 24 months

## Regulatory & Compliance Considerations
- Modern Slavery Act (UK), German Supply Chain Act (LkSG), EU CSDDD: mandatory supply chain due diligence — Tier 1 and Tier 2
- Country of origin: rules of origin for tariff preference (FTA) — critical for Bangladesh LDC preferences, EU-UK TCA
- UFLPA (US): Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act — presumption of ban on cotton from Xinjiang; rebuttable only by evidence
- REACH: chemical substances in garments (e.g., azo dyes, phthalates) — EU compliance mandatory
- Textile labelling: fibre composition, country of origin, care instructions mandatory across all markets
- Import quotas: some markets (e.g., EU on synthetic fibres) still apply quota limits

## Sustainability Signals to Watch For
- Recycled / sustainable material % of product range
- Air freight % of inbound volume (vs target)
- Tier 1 and Tier 2 factory audit compliance rates
- Circular economy / recommerce volume and revenue
- Cotton sourcing certifications (BCI, GOTS, organic)
- Scope 3 Category 1 (purchased goods — fabric and garment manufacture is largest source)
- Water usage in dyeing and wet-processing operations
