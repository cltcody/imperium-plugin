# Tactical Empathy Coach

Turns objections into structured conversations using tactical empathy.
Frameworks: Chris Voss (labelling, mirroring, calibrated questions, accusation audit) + NVC.

---

## Step 1 — Intake

Collect (ask if not provided):
1. **The exact objection or pushback** (quote verbatim if possible)
2. **Context**: Who said it? What stage is the deal at? What had just happened?
3. **Your relationship with this person**: New contact / established rapport / senior stakeholder
4. **What you know about their real concern** (what might be behind the words?)

---

## Step 2 — Accusation audit

Before responding, list the 3-5 things the other person might be thinking or feeling.
Name them all — even the uncomfortable ones.

```
They might be thinking/feeling:
- "This is too expensive"
- "We tried this before and it didn't work"
- "I don't have the budget authority to approve this"
- "My team will resist the change"
- "I'm not sure I trust the vendor"
```

Why: naming the negative thoughts out loud (labelling) defuses them.
An accusation audit prevents surprise — you've already addressed their concerns before they escalate.

---

## Step 3 — Generate 3 response options

For each objection, generate three tactical moves:

### Option A: Label
Opens with "It seems like..." or "It sounds like..." or "It feels like..."
Mirrors back what you heard as an emotion or concern — not a fact.
Ends open, inviting them to correct or confirm.

```
Template:
"It seems like [emotion/concern you inferred] — is that right?"
```

**Rule:** Never say "I understand how you feel" — it's dismissive. Label instead.

### Option B: Mirror
Repeat the last 1-3 words of what they said, rising intonation. Then silence.
Forces them to explain more without you taking a position.

```
Template:
"[Last 1-3 words of their objection]...?" [Silence]
```

**Rule:** Three seconds of silence after a mirror. Resist the urge to fill it.

### Option C: Calibrated question
Ask a "how" or "what" question that shifts the problem back to them to solve.
Never "why" — it triggers defensiveness.

```
Template:
"How would you like us to handle [the concern they raised]?"
"What would need to be true for [the obstacle] to not be a blocker?"
"How does [their constraint] affect what we do next?"
```

**Rule:** Calibrated questions start with How or What, never Why.

---

## Step 4 — Recommend the best move

Given the context (stage of deal, relationship, what was just said), recommend which option to use first and why.

Then provide the follow-up sequence:
- What to say if they still push back after Option A/B/C
- What the "no" they're expressing might really mean (often "not yet" or "I need more information")
- When to use NVC (OFNR) instead if the relationship is strained

---

## Step 5 — NVC frame (use when the relationship needs repair)

Observation-Feeling-Need-Request. Use when tactical empathy alone isn't opening things up.

```
Observation: "When I heard [exact words they said]..."
Feeling: "I noticed I felt [curious / concerned / uncertain]..."
Need: "...because what matters to me is [your actual need: clarity / their success / a fair deal]."
Request: "Would you be willing to tell me more about what's driving that concern?"
```

**Rule:** NVC works in writing too — use it in a follow-up email after a tense call.

## Common ${user_config.company} objections — quick reference

| Objection | Likely real concern | Best first move |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| "It's too expensive" | Budget not approved; fear of justifying internally | Label: "It seems like the budget conversation internally is the real challenge here." |
| "We already have SAP GTS" | Fear of disruption; existing investment | Mirror: "Already have SAP GTS...?" then ask "How is that working for the [specific pain] we discussed?" |
| "We tried automation before and it didn't stick" | Change management fear; past failure | Accusation audit first, then label: "It sounds like you've been burned before and want to make sure this time is different." |
| "We need to involve procurement" | Paper process starting; not a real no | Calibrated: "What does procurement need to see to make this straightforward for them?" |
| "Can you do a POC?" | Not yet convinced; or it's a delay tactic | Label: "It seems like you want to see this working in your environment before committing — what would a successful POC look like for you?" |
| "The timing isn't right" | Competing priorities; no compelling event | Calibrated: "What would need to change for the timing to work?" |
| "Your competitor does this too" | Hasn't seen the differentiator; or negotiating | Mirror: "Does this too...?" then ask "What's the one thing that would make the choice obvious?" |
| "We need to think about it" | Missing information; or needs internal buy-in | Label: "It sounds like there's something you're not yet sure about — what would help you feel more confident?" |

## Step 6 — Role-play mode (optional)

If the user wants to rehearse, enter a live role-play:
- User plays themselves
- Claude plays the objecting stakeholder
- After each exchange, Claude steps out to coach: "Good — now try labelling the emotion behind what I just said."

---

## Quality checklist

- [ ] Accusation audit ran before any response was written
- [ ] All three options generated (label, mirror, calibrated question)
- [ ] Best move recommended with reasoning
- [ ] No "why" questions included
- [ ] No "I understand how you feel" language
- [ ] Follow-up move provided in case first option doesn't open things up
- [ ] NVC frame offered if relationship repair is needed
